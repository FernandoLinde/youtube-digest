Brazil

March’s IPCA-15 will be released on Thursday (Mar 26). Our preliminary call for March’s IPCA-15 stands at 0.25%, with no market median available yet. Twelve-month inflation should ease to 3.70% (from 4.10% in January), the lowest level since May-24 (3.70%). Qualitatively, the print should show mixed signals, with underlying services standing out: 3M SAAR should decelerate from 5.2% to 4.9%, while labor-intensive services should slow from 7.3% to 7.0%. Meanwhile, the 3M SAAR of the average of core measures is projected to rise from 4.3% to 4.6%. Relative to February’s IPCA-15 (0.84%), we expect more benign regulated inflation (0.10% vs. 0.69%), driven by gasoline deflation (-0.30% vs. 1.30%). Industrial goods should also post a smaller increase (0.23% vs. 0.44%), reflecting some cooling in personal care (0.48% vs. 0.91%). Meanwhile, services should post a lower reading (0.11% vs. 1.50%), driven by the fading of yearly tuition hikes (0.00% vs. 6.18%) and by less pressured underlying services (0.41% vs. 0.65%).

February’s PNAD will be released on Friday (Mar 27). We expect the unemployment rate at 5.6% (after 5.4% in January). The increase is driven by the usual seasonal rise in unemployment at the start of the year. According to our seasonal adjustment, the unemployment rate would remain stable around 5.3%, and could rise to 5.4% depending on the employment and labor-force prints (we project both to increase in line with the latest release). A key point to monitor is the dynamics of real wages, which have accelerated in recent months, reinforcing the view of a still-very-tight labor market. Over the next few quarters, we expect a marginal increase in the unemployment rate due to some recovery in the participation rate, but with unemployment remaining well below the NAIRU.

This week, federal tax revenue data for February is expected to be released. We forecast a total of R$223.5bn, equivalent to a real increase of 4.7% y/y in the 2M26 period and 6.3% y/y in the interannual comparison. This time, in addition to taxes linked to the labor market, consumption-related taxes are also expected to gain traction, playing an important role in the observed growth for the month.

At the beginning of the week, the first bimonthly report of 2026 will also be published. We expect a blocking slightly below R$5 billion and no contingency. The revision of oil prices and the new export tax should support revenues. The blocking has a precautionary nature, with no relevant expenditure pressure, and the diesel subsidy will be implemented via extraordinary credit, outside the spending cap.

We expect the current account (CA) deficit to total US$5.3bn in February, lower than the deficit observed in the same month last year (US$10.2bn). This reduction will be driven by a higher trade balance (US$3.6bn, compared to a deficit of US$1.1bn in February 2025), as well as lower income expenditures (due to lower remittances of profits and dividends, mainly). FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) is expected to remain robust, totaling US$7.0bn in the month. In the LTM period, the CA deficit should continue to narrow, totaling US$62.6bn, comfortably financed by FDI, which is expected to reach US$76.1bn over the same period.

Chile

The BCCh’s monetary policy meeting will take place on Tuesday, and we expect the Board to keep the MPR at 4.5%. The focus of interest is not on the Board’s decision itself – where the high uncertainty in the external scenario fully justifies maintaining the rate – but rather on how the IPoM projections and forward guidance will change amid the sharp rise in oil prices. Using the inflation response from the BCCh’s XMAS model to an oil shock, we estimate that the inflation projection for 4Q26 could rise to around 4-4.5%, before sharply decelerating to 2.5-3% by 4Q27. Despite this significant increase in inflation, we expect the Board to adopt a wait-and-see stance given the high uncertainty surrounding the conflict in Iran, the consequent rise in oil prices, the behavior of global financial markets, and their effects on the Chilean economy. Since the conflict is very recent, we do not expect the Board to be very definitive about its monetary policy strategy going forward, likely indicating that the policy response will depend on the depth and persistence of the ongoing shock. In this context, the forward guidance delivered in the IPoM – scheduled for release next Wednesday – should be read with caution, placing less emphasis on the baseline scenario and more weight on the alternative scenarios considered to shape the boundaries of the MPR corridor. Brazil’s fiscal imbalance is largely rooted in the rapid growth of mandatory spending. In this report, we focus on the role of expenditure indexation, which affects roughly 45% of federal primary spending. We estimate that the changes introduced by the Transition Amendment to indexation rules will increase federal primary spending by R$215bn by 2026, relative to a counterfactual scenario with more moderate rules. This reflects primarily the reinstatement of the minimum wage adjustment rule, the retroactive recalculation of health and education spending floors with indexation to revenue, and the expansion of congressional earmarks. Under this scenario, real primary spending growth reaches 21% over the period, compared with 11% in the counterfactual. For the 2027–2034 period, we simulate a set of changes to indexation rules, including linking social benefits to inflation, adjusting health and education floors by inflation and population growth, partially de-linking Fundeb and the FCDF, and keeping congressional earmarks flat in nominal terms. Under these assumptions, primary spending would be R$355bn lower by 2034, with the primary balance reaching 2% of GDP, compared with 0.5% under current rules, and real spending growth slowing to 9%, versus 19% if current rules remain unchanged. In this scenario, assuming a 4.5% real interest rate, public debt would stabilize at around 88% of GDP by 2030. (Full story here) <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/118823> 

Currently, there are two proposed constitutional amendments (PEC 8/2025 and PEC 221/2019) under consideration in Congress aimed at reducing the maximum working week and establishing a minimum of two days of rest per week in Brazil; The purpose of this note is to provide estimates of the magnitude of the supply shock such a measure may represent, as well as its distribution across sectors, firm types, locations, and worker characteristics. The 2024 data (RAIS) show an average workload of 42.0 hours per week, with a high concentration (75.5%) of employment contracts at the constitutional maximum of 44 weekly hours. We estimate that lowering this limit to 36 weekly hours (40) would lead to a direct reduction of 16.1% (7.7%) in the total number of hours worked in the country. Wages paid for the hours no longer worked — since wages cannot be reduced — would amount to 14.6% (6.5%) of firms’ total wage bill. Using sectoral cost-structure data, we estimate that the average impact would be a 4.7% increase in firms’ total costs, ranging from 0.5% (extractive industries) to 8.7% (administrative & support services) if the maximum workload is reduced to 36 hours per week; and 2.1% on average, ranging from 0.2% (extractive industries) to 4.1% (administrative & support services) if the limit is changed to 40 hours per week. The simulations show that smaller firms are the most affected. In a hypothetical exercise assuming that total hours worked do not change and that firms replace the lost hours at an additional cost through: (1) overtime payments; (2) hiring in the informal sector; and/or (3) hiring employees with shorter working hours, the estimated effects using 2024 data range between 11.5% and 21.9% of total labor costs under a shift to 36 hours (or between 5.1% and 9.7% under a shift to 40 weekly hours). Naturally, we do not know what the new equilibrium will look like under the likely change in the maximum contracted hours. One should expect changes not only in the unit cost of labor hours, but also in labor costs in the informal sector and in the hourly income differential across workers with different workloads, making this a worthwhile topic for future research. Chile

GDP grew 2.5% in 2025, somewhat above the 2.3% previously suggested by IMACEC figures. While 4Q25 GDP growth was 1.6% y/y, modestly below the 1.7% implied in the IMACEC, the upward revision of previous quarterly figures led to higher average growth for the year (tables 1-2). We also note that 2024 growth was revised up to 2.8%, from a previous 2.6%. Regarding the expenditure breakdown of 2025 GDP growth, we highlight the 7% growth of fixed investment, followed by a 2.7% increase in private consumption. It is also relevant to note that while the economy decelerated in q/q terms in 2H25, this was mostly due to the mining sector, while non-mining GDP expanded at a pace of 0.5-0.6% q/q during the period, which is consistent with an economy growing  around its potential rate. Looking forward, the dynamism shown by the economy is consistent with our view of GDP growing around 2.5% in 2026. Notwithstanding, the material increase in the price of oil since the outbreak of the war in Iran puts a downward bias on this forecast. (Full story here) <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/118791> 

The outbreak of the war in Iran on February 28 has been a game changer for global financial markets, and the CLP has been no exception. The currency is currently trading around 915, about 6.4% weaker than at the time of our previous report. Nevertheless, this depreciation is far from idiosyncratic, as it is fully explained by the risk-off environment that has taken hold of global financial markets, with uncertainty over the scope and consequences of the war in Iran dominating the agenda. The sharp increase in oil prices, the broad-based appreciation of the USD, and the rise in global financial risk indicators such as the VIX all help explain the CLP’s depreciation. Consistent with this, our FX model indicates that the CLP has remained fairly priced throughout this period of heightened volatility. Unsurprisingly, this implies that future movements in the CLP will be closely tied to the evolution of the war in Iran, its implications for the global economy, and the uncertainty surrounding it. Given that the domestic backdrop remains constructive and copper prices have stayed elevated, we believe the CLP should strengthen once clearer signs emerge that the war in Iran is approaching an end. Colombia

According to DANE, imports reached US$5.9bn CIF in Jan-26, up 9.7% y/y (vs. US$5.4bn in Jan-25), driven mainly by manufacture (+14.1% y/y; +10.5p.p. contribution), particularly machinery and transport equipment. Agricultural goods, food and beverages also contributed positively (+13.2% y/y; +1.9p.p.), while fuels and extractives declined (-25.3% y/y; -2.7p.p.), partially offsetting the headline print. In terms of composition, manufactures accounted for 77.7% of total imports, followed by agriculture (14.9%) and fuels (7.3%).

As per DANE, the January trade deficit was to US$1.3bn FOB (vs. near balance at ~US$1.2mn a year earlier). Imports accounted for US$5.6bn, while exports clocked at US$4.3bn. Banrep warned that the draft decree allowing the transfer of COP25tn in pension savings from private funds (AFPs) to Colpensiones would likely be illegal, as it contradicts Law 2381/2024, which permits regime switching but explicitly requires that assets remain managed by AFPs until pension rights are realized; the proposal instead mandates the transfer of all balances (capital + returns) within 15 business days, effectively exceeding regulatory powers and altering the law’s scope. The central bank also highlighted that the reform is partially suspended by the Constitutional Court and that the contributory pillar fund is not yet operational, reinforcing the need to maintain current transitional rules. Additionally, Banrep flagged potential market risks, noting that a forced and rapid reallocation, given AFPs’ heavy exposure to TES, could disrupt local debt markets and financial stability.

According to DANE, Colombia’s ISE grew 1.55% y/y in January 2026, broadly in line with late-2025 dynamics, while the seasonally adjusted index fell 0.07% m/m, suggesting limited momentum at the start of the year. The breakdown shows a persistent divergence across sectors: services remained the main driver (+2.7% y/y), supported by public and social services as well as commerce-related activities, while primary activities contracted (-2.4% y/y) amid weakness in mining and agriculture, and secondary sectors also declined (-1.0% y/y) reflecting still-soft manufacturing and construction .

On March 18, 2026, 23 members of Colombia’s House of Representatives filed a motion of censure against Health Minister Guillermo Alfonso Jaramillo, citing political accountability for a systemic deterioration in healthcare delivery, evidenced by rising legal claims, treatment disruptions, medication backlogs, and persistent delays in service provision, and formally requested the scheduling of a congressional debate to assess sector governance, arguing that the lack of structural and proportional policy responses reflects a failure in ministerial oversight and undermines the effective guarantee of fundamental health rights.

Following the government’s update of the 2026 Financial Plan, CARF maintains its overall assessment of the fiscal outlook, incorporating only minor revisions to its baseline scenario, including higher projected revenues (+COP0.7tn) and expenditures (+COP1.9tn). These adjustments result in a slightly higher primary deficit estimate for 2026 (3.7% of GDP) under CARF’s no-adjustment scenario, broadly reaffirming the committee’s view that fiscal pressures remain elevated. Importantly, the update explicitly incorporates the government’s revised fiscal path, yet CARF does not change its stance on credibility, stressing that the mechanisms to achieve the proposed expenditure reduction remain unclear and estimating a fiscal shortfall of COP32.1tn (~1.6% of GDP) relative to official targets.

Daniel Noboa and Gustavo Petro agreed to resume negotiations within the Andean Community to contain a growing trade dispute that had raised fears of a commercial war between Ecuador and Colombia, even as diplomatic tensions intensified after Petro denounced alleged cross-border bombings, accusations that Noboa rejected, highlighting a fragile effort to separate economic cooperation from political and security disagreements.

The March BanRep Monthly Expectations Survey (EME) points to inflation of 0.67% m/m (5.45% y/y) for March, below BTG Pactual’s forecast of 0.80% m/m (5.58% y/y). Medium-term inflation expectations remained broadly stable, with Dec-26 expectations edging down slightly to 6.18% (from 6.19%; BTG: 6.4%) and Dec-27 expectations declining to 4.75% (from 4.77%; BTG: 4.3%). At the monetary policy horizon, expectations were mixed: 12-month inflation rose to 5.70% (from 5.66%), while 24-month expectations declined to 4.36% (from 4.44%), indicating limited progress toward the 3% target but a gradual improvement in longer horizons. On the rates side, respondents see the policy rate at 11.01% in March (BTG: 11.25%), while the forward path shifted somewhat higher, with Dec-26 expectations rising to 11.77% (from 11.66%; BTG: 12.25%) and Dec-27 increasing to 9.96% (from 9.81%; BTG: 10.75%.

According to the Monthly Manufacturing Survey (EMMET), real manufacturing production declined 0.5% y/y in January 2026, while real sales fell 0.7% y/y and employment edged up 0.1% y/y, suggesting a weak start to the year for the industrial sector. The report indicates that 22 out of 39 manufacturing activities registered negative annual production growth, subtracting 2.9pp from total output, while 17 subsectors posted positive contributions adding 2.4pp, highlighting a broadly mixed sectoral performance. From a territorial perspective, 9 out of 14 departments recorded declines in industrial production, with notable contractions in Tolima (-16.8%), Córdoba (-10.1%), and Cauca (-7.1%), while some regions such as Santander (+11.1%) and Caldas (+3.8%) showed stronger performance.

According to DANE’s Industrial Production Index, industrial output declined 0.5% y/y in January 2026, marking a slight contraction in overall activity. By sector, mining and quarrying fell 4.0% y/y and manufacturing declined 0.5% y/y, weighing on the headline index. In contrast, electricity and gas supply increased 2.7% y/yand water supply rose 3.7% y/y, partially offsetting the weakness in other segments. At a more disaggregated level, 15 out of 26 industrial activities recorded negative annual growth, subtracting 2.2pp from the overall variation, while 11 activities expanded, contributing 1.7pp, highlighting the mixed sectoral performance at the start of the year.

According to the Monthly Retail Trade Survey (EMC), real retail sales (including vehicles) increased 7.8% y/y in January 2026, while employment in the sector rose 2.0% y/y, confirming a positive start to the year for domestic demand. Excluding fuel sales, retail growth was even stronger at 10.9% y/y, highlighting the broad-based nature of the expansion. The main positive contributions came from vehicle sales (+30.5% y/y), information and telecommunications equipment (+38.0%), and audio-video equipment (+36.6%), reflecting robust demand for durable goods. By contrast, fuel sales (-5.1%) and hardware-related items (-2.2%) partially offset the overall expansion. Overall, the data point to a continued recovery in retail activity, with durable goods leading the expansion at the start of the year.

According to the Monthly Services Survey (EMS), 15 out of 18 service subsectors recorded positive annual growth in nominal revenues in January 2026, reflecting broad-based expansion across the sector. Among the strongest performers were employment, security and building services (+21.4% y/y revenues), film and television production (+19.1%), and other entertainment services (+11.5%), highlighting robust demand in several business and leisure-related activities. However, performance was uneven across segments, with publishing (-11.5%), call centers (-9.6%), and storage and transport-related services (-5.6%) registering declines in revenues. Labor market dynamics were mixed, with 8 out of 18 subsectors posting employment gains, while all service subsectors recorded increases in average wages, pointing to persistent cost pressures in the sector.

According to the Monthly Accommodation Survey (EMA), hotel activity started 2026 on a weaker footing. In January, real revenues in the accommodation sector contracted 4.1% y/y, mainly driven by declines in Bogotá (-9.2%), Cartagena (-6.7%), and San Andrés (-8.8%). Employment in the sector also softened, with occupied personnel falling 3.1% Y/y, reflecting particularly sharp declines in San Andrés (-11.1%), the Caribbean region (-3.4%), and Bogotá (-2.5%). In contrast, real wages rose 9.9% y/y, led by increases in the Caribbean region (14.5%), Cartagena (7.7%), and Bogotá (10.0%), suggesting continued cost pressures despite weaker activity. Meanwhile, national hotel occupancy reached 49.7% in January, slightly below the 50.5% recorded a year earlier, with leisure travel remaining the main driver of demand.

Peru

Peru’s interim president José María Balcázar reshuffled his cabinet just three weeks after appointing it on February 24, replacing six ministers, including naming retired military officer Luis Arroyo as prime minister, in a bid to secure congressional confidence ahead of upcoming general elections; the new appointees are largely technocrats and former senior officials (e.g., ex-vice ministers and sector specialists) tasked with stabilizing governance in the short term, and are expected to serve through the remainder of the transitional administration until the next elected government takes office. 

With less than one month before the April 12, 2026 general elections, the latest Datum survey (Mar 6–10) points to a highly fragmented and fluid electoral landscape, with Rafael López Aliaga (11.4%) marginally ahead of Keiko Fujimori (10.9%), while Alfonso López Chau (Ahora Nación) consolidates a standalone third position at 6.5%, breaking from prior statistical ties with César Acuña and Carlos Álvarez, after gaining ~1.5pp relative to the previous poll; his recent uptick appears to be driven by stronger performance in regional constituencies and coincides with a contraction in the share of undecided voters (15.2%), although the combined weight of blank/invalid ballots (21.5%) and residual indecision continues to signal low voter anchoring and significant scope for late-stage vote reallocation. 

Napoleón Becerra, a 61-year-old Peruvian presidential candidate and founder of the Workers and Entrepreneurs Party (PTE), died in a traffic accident on March 15 while traveling to a campaign event in Ayacucho; under electoral law, his death nullifies the presidential ticket he headed, meaning his running mates, first vice-presidential candidate Winston Huamán and second vice-presidential candidate Nélida Juliana Cuayla, cannot remain in the race and the entire ticket is effectively removed from the 2026 election.

The result was driven by positive prints in 11 of 14 sectors. In m/m (s.a.) terms, national production increased by 0.63%. In February 2026, according to Ipsos, Peru’s Consumer Confidence Index (CCI) stood at 49.7 points, edging up +1.5 pts m/m from January (48.2). The index remains below but near the neutral 50 threshold, signaling continued mild pessimism, though with relative stability at the start of 2026.
