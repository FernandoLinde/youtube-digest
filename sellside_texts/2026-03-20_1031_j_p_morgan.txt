Readthrough from India’s Semaglutide Patent Expiry to Brazil 


We would like to flag the report from our colleagues based in India: Latika Chopra, CFA and Bansi Desai, CFA (link <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12833371&ad=4072023978&h=YSidsdS&sig=XGJI2aMfXMyzloqyLAxGviFWwdA&v=2&isig=OFtAk7Pycgu8JJB94uatgAGLrp7&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/-emau7qo/GPS-5239429-0/5c21334f-6c41-4c28-a93c-4c4447925df1> ) highlighting the impacts of Semaglutide patent expiry on Indian healthcare and consumer sectors. In this context, it is worth flagging that the same patent expired today in Brazil but the local regulator, ANVISA, is yet to grant licenses for the registration of generic and branded generic alternatives. Below are the main highlights. 

*	Price is the throttle. In India, semaglutide going off‑patent is cutting monthly costs by roughly 50–60% as generics enter (from ~Rs10–20k//US$106-214 toward ~Rs3–5k//US$32-53), which materially improves access but still concentrates uptake among affluent, urban consumers given out‑of‑pocket burden. 
*	Demand curve: urban first, modest penetration. Even with lower prices, modeled compliance (lower for obesity than Type‑2 diabetes) implies low‑single‑digit penetration near term with negligible rural adoption—demand anchored in big cities and higher incomes; watch how payer coverage and retail discounts in LatAm could bend this curve faster or slower. 
*	Elasticity matters. India’s team models GLP‑1 users rising from ~120k (Feb’26) to ~2.5mn/5.3mn by 2030/2035 as prices fall—clear evidence that sustained price erosion unlocks step‑ups in demand; if LatAm sees similar generic dynamics and partial reimbursement, expect a non‑linear adoption ramp from a small base. 
*	Consumption signal resilience. Global category reads show smaller portions and a pivot from full‑sugar toward diet/hydration/protein; quantified guidance is modest—e.g., 0.5–1.5% 10‑yr volume drag for snacks—suggesting demand effects trail pricing‑led adoption and remain manageable at low penetration levels.
