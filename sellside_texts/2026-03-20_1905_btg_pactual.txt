Brazil

Brazil’s fiscal imbalance is largely rooted in the rapid growth of mandatory spending. In this report, we focus on the role of expenditure indexation, which affects roughly 45% of federal primary spending. We estimate that the changes introduced by the Transition Amendment to indexation rules will increase federal primary spending by R$215bn by 2026, relative to a counterfactual scenario with more moderate rules. This reflects primarily the reinstatement of the minimum wage adjustment rule, the retroactive recalculation of health and education spending floors with indexation to revenue, and the expansion of congressional earmarks. Under this scenario, real primary spending growth reaches 21% over the period, compared with 11% in the counterfactual. For the 2027–2034 period, we simulate a set of changes to indexation rules, including linking social benefits to inflation, adjusting health and education floors by inflation and population growth, partially de-linking Fundeb and the FCDF, and keeping congressional earmarks flat in nominal terms. Under these assumptions, primary spending would be R$355bn lower by 2034, with the primary balance reaching 2% of GDP, compared with 0.5% under current rules, and real spending growth slowing to 9%, versus 19% if current rules remain unchanged. In this scenario, assuming a 4.5% real interest rate, public debt would stabilize at around 88% of GDP by 2030. (Full story here) <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/118823> 

Argentina

The unemployment rate jumped from 6.6% in Q3 to 7.5% in the last quarter of 2025. The jobless rate also grew significantly y/y coming from 6.4% in the last quarter of 2024. In the last year, the participation rate fell marginally from 48.8% to 48.6%, but the employment rate declined from 45.7% to just 45%. This rate is not as bad as it may look, as the 2024 employment rate was the highest on record for the fourth quarter, and 2025’s is the second highest. Also, the 6.6% unemployment rate in Q3 was surprisingly low given the state of the economy, with all the electoral turmoil. Chile

Market participants now expect March inflation to reach 0.6% m/m, representing a 0.2pp increase relative to the previous survey. The BCCh released the results of the pre-March monetary policy meeting Traders Survey, showing that short-term inflation expectations stand at 0.6% m/m for March, followed by 0.5% m/m in April and 0.2% m/m in May. Longer-term expectations have risen notably, with the one-year horizon increasing from 2.9% y/y to 3.5% y/y, while the two-year horizon stands at 3.1% y/y. Regarding monetary policy, participants no longer anticipate a rate cut at the March 24 meeting and expect the MPR to remain at 4.5% over the next four monetary policy meetings. At the 12-month horizon, the policy rate is projected at 4.25%, before returning to 4.5% at the 24-month horizon. In the foreign exchange market, expectations point to the CLP trading around 910 CLP/USD over the next seven days, followed by a moderate appreciation toward approximately 900 CLP/USD over the subsequent 28-day horizon.

Colombia

According to DANE, imports reached US$5.9bn CIF in Jan-26, up 9.7% y/y (vs. US$5.4bn in Jan-25), driven mainly by manufacture (+14.1% y/y; +10.5p.p. contribution), particularly machinery and transport equipment. Agricultural goods, food and beverages also contributed positively (+13.2% y/y; +1.9p.p.), while fuels and extractives declined (-25.3% y/y; -2.7p.p.), partially offsetting the headline print. In terms of composition, manufactures accounted for 77.7% of total imports, followed by agriculture (14.9%) and fuels (7.3%).

As per DANE, the January trade deficit was to US$1.3bn FOB (vs. near balance at ~US$1.2mn a year earlier). Imports accounted for US$5.6bn, while exports clocked at US$4.3bn.

Banrep warned that the draft decree allowing the transfer of COP25tn in pension savings from private funds (AFPs) to Colpensiones would likely be illegal, as it contradicts Law 2381/2024, which permits regime switching but explicitly requires that assets remain managed by AFPs until pension rights are realized; the proposal instead mandates the transfer of all balances (capital + returns) within 15 business days, effectively exceeding regulatory powers and altering the law’s scope.
