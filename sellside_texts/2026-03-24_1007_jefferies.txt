Macro

Heading into the second half of March investors appear well hedged with the put/call ratio near 1-yr highs and elevated short interest across ETFs. As such, the desk favors supply chain related hedges because even if tensions cool, damage to supply chains will take time to amend. Strikes on Qatar’s Ras Laffan wiped out ~17% of the regions LNG capacity. For context, Qatar produces roughly 20% of global LNG supply. Furthermore, the doubling of fuel oil threatens the transportation supply. Companies that produce and transport LNG stand to benefit from these bottlenecks. Chemical producers will also likely be disrupted by higher oil and naphtha prices as well as shipping delays. Fortunately for DOW, they’re relatively better positioned versus peers because its inputs are tied to US light feedstocks.

The key takeaway here is how TSLA is pricing its fully self-driving vs how LMND is pricing

*	TSLA – is basically adding ‘beta’ to both sides of its AV risk experience: enabling fully self driving does not make the difference:

	*	If the vehicle performs better with fully self driving engaged = better pricing
	*	But also vice versa, as gauged through hard braking and other risk signals
	*	Unlike traditional telematics it seems to effectively make autonomous continuously earn better price through observed behavior and could in theory notably reward autonomy structurally as the software delivers cleaner driving in the data.
	*	AA also notes - For drivers who use the same routes on a regular basis, the savings from Tesla could be quite significant as FSD learns. TSLA has made the filings in FL and IN and changes are policy wide, not just on per-mile coverages.

*	LMND – model seems to instead be offering an immediate pricing discount to fully self driving right out of the gate

	*	This could be a sales tactic – AA describes as ‘cross subsidization of poor autonomy uses’
	*	Or perhaps just targeted to lower miles driven users
	*	AA notes – While LMND's FSD product highlights how autonomy can improve safety on average (using data from TSLA), we believe that tying discounts to assumed benefits rather than observed outcomes risks rising LRs over time when compared to other approaches.
