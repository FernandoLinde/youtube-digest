Feedback from YDUQS NDR: navigating a softer cycle

This week, we hosted a non-deal roadshow with YDUQS in Rio, with CFO Alexandre Aquino and IR Director Bernardo Lobão. Overall, the tone was more cautious than peers, reflecting a still challenging intake backdrop in the Estácio and Wyden brands, as expected in an election year. While management acknowledged softer market conditions, the company continues to outperform the industry on a relative basis and reiterated expectations for mid-single digit revenue growth in 2026, supported by solid retention and gradual mix improvement. Free cash flow generation remains a key focus, with management signaling potential upside vs. 2025.

Intake: weak market, but relative outperformance

Relative to Hoper’s January–February market data (an education consultancy), YDUQS has been outperforming the industry, despite declining ~7% on a consolidated basis. Performance across segments remains mixed at the company level: on-campus intake has been slightly weaker than expected (-5% y/y), although excluding PROUNI, on-campus paying students are down only ~1% y/y, as prior cycles benefited from excess PROUNI intake. Distance learning continues to decline (-37%), while hybrid stands out as the fastest-growing segment (+74%). Growth in on-campus and hybrid is partially offsetting the decline in distance learning, although expansion has been more concentrated in partner hubs, which carry lower ticket levels. On the positive side, retention remains strong, and the company does not expect a decline in the student base in 1Q; as such, intake revenues should come broadly in line with 1Q25.

Margins: mix pressure, partly offset by better credit

Looking ahead, margins are expected to face some pressure in 2026, mainly reflecting mix effects from stronger growth in on-campus and hybrid segments. That said, management expects nominal EBITDA per student to continue expanding, helping sustain overall profitability. Importantly, part of this dynamic should be offset by continued improvements in credit quality, with PDA expected to decline by ~1p.p., following similar gains seen in 2025. The impact from the new regulatory framework should remain limited (~0.3% of net revenues in 2026 and ~0.7% in 2027), as prior cost adjustments help mitigate potential pressures.

Cash generation: outlook improving y/y

Management indicated that free cash flow in 2026 should be at least in line with 2025, with upside potential. Early trends are encouraging, with January and February cash generation already above 1Q25 levels (R$250mn). The outlook is supported by a combination of lower non-recurring expenses (expected below R$60mn in 2026), gradual EBITDA growth (mid-single digit), better financial results (lower interest rates), continued improvement in PDA, and lower adhesion to DIS (deferral tuition program), which supports cash collection dynamics. Importantly, these tailwinds should more than offset the decline in non-recurring cash inflows from PraValer, which are expected to fall from ~R$90mn in 2025 to ~R$30mn in 2026, following changes in the receivables model.

Premium: key segments remain supportive

Within premium segments, management highlighted both medicine and IBMEC as relevant growth drivers. In medicine, demand remains strong, although competition has increased in some regions, making the current intake cycle somewhat more challenging. The expansion of FIES (with a higher ticket cap) has supported demand but weighs on reported ticket levels; still, management views the economics as broadly neutral in NPV terms. On regulation, ENAMED could impact ~150–200 seats in the current cycle (2026.2). At the same time, IBMEC continues to show strong improvements in profitability, supported by better capacity utilization and pricing optimization (notably in Rio), with further expansion opportunities in executive education, B2B offerings and postgraduate programs. Overall, premium segments are expected to grow at double-digit rates, helping offset softer trends in traditional undergrad programs.

Capital allocation: buybacks back in focus

The company reiterated a selective approach to M&A, particularly in segments such as medicine, with deleveraging remaining a key priority. On liability management, the company has already improved its cost of debt (recent issuances at ~CDI +0.7%) and still sees room for further optimization. Management expects leverage to gradually decline, approaching ~1.0x ND/EBITDA by end-2027. In this context, capital allocation may increasingly shift toward shareholder returns, either through a revision of the dividend policy or share buybacks. In fact, the company recently announced a new R$100mn buyback program (~9.9mn shares, or ~4% of free float), to be executed over the next 18 months.

Putting things into perspective: softer trends, but no change to the story

We maintain our Buy rating on YDUQS, supported by a still compelling ~18% 2026 FCF yield. While risks to our estimates have increased since Q4 results, the overall message from management was more constructive than expected. In our view, the softer intake should be seen in the context of a weak market backdrop, with YDUQS still outperforming peers and preserving solid cash generation dynamics.
