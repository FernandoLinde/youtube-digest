Guilherme Levy, Bruno Montanari, Martijn Rats, CFA, Thiago S Casqueiro, Alice Bergier Winograd, Luiza B Belem	 
March 22, 2026

	
Last week we met with a variety of majors, services providers, local companies and government entities in our site visits to Brazil and Argentina. Below are our key takeaways. 

*	No supply shortage alarms in Brazil to this point, such as those we are seeing in some Asian countries on the back of the closure of Hormuz. Argentina could emerge as a relative winner in the context of recent Middle East developments, as it is self-sufficient in crude and fuels, unlike Brazil, which imports ~30% of its diesel consumption.
*	The upstream M&A market is tight, with a lack of oil assets for sale, particularly in Brazil. Hence, companies are focussed on organic growth opportunities and exploration. However, changes to fiscal terms (dividend tax in 2025 and the reintroduction of temporary oil export tax in 2026) weigh on the country’s attractiveness for international investments.
*	FPSO providers (SBM, Modec) have a busy pipeline of current work and new orders coming in. No particular concerns about competition from pure shipyards, as their value proposition is different. The way to protect against inflation is by better managing the procurement of long-lead items when a new project is FID’d. No delays to sanctioning of SEAP FPSOs expected because of current market volatility.
*	Companies in Argentina racing to monetize natural gas production from shale (with nearing FIDs on NGLs, LNG, and urea). Current cost to drill and complete wells in Vaca Muerta ~30% higher than US shale plays — of which 1/3 structural — leaving room to further bring costs down and enhance IRRs.
