Investors are cautious; Appear more inclined to trim than bottom-fish

Over the past few days, my colleague Cesar Perez and I have met investors in London, and the feedback is quite cautious. Needless to say, given the ongoing correction and risk off in markets, it was inevitable that discussions would initially turn to the investment landscape for commodities. The general feedback from investors here is bearish, with many still expecting further downside and looking to reduce exposure rather than bottom fish. The main concern is that the war in Iran poses serious (long-lasting) risks to energy markets, global supply chains, monetary policy and ultimately growth, with very little visibility on how long it may last. After months of spectacular performance in (some) commodities, particularly copper and gold stocks, the overall impression is that risks in the space remain elevated given valuations. We sensed the most interest in Vale by far, with most recognizing the recent bottom up improvements. Investors appear to be closely monitoring gold and copper stocks amid the sell off, but still lack conviction to pull the trigger. Investors remain broadly bearish on pulp and we saw very limited interest in steel.

Vale dominated discussions; The more “defensive” one?

Expectedly, discussions surrounding Vale dominated most of our air time in meetings. The FCF yield gap to Australians, the fact that the C1 cost gap has been eliminated, and capital allocation simplicity are seen as key differentiating factors. We also attribute this interest to the favorable bottom up environment at the company, with management delivering on several fronts operationally, the emerging copper growth theme, and still very decent cash return potential (8-9%). In this messy global context, several agree with the view that Vale is increasingly seen as a lower beta, more defensive story, with iron ore prices near US$110/t and the company largely hedged on freight through long term contracts and around 75% bunker hedges. In copper, investors appear willing to assess growth prospects through 2030 to around 450-500 kt from 380ktpa, driven by more “tangible” projects in Brazil, but still need time to better understand the path to 700kt by 2035. On iron ore, there are concerns that Chinese data is weaker than expected (CSP under reporting cited as a theme), but most agree with our call that Simandou’s ramp up is slow in 2026 and largely a non event, representing around 1% of seaborne supply.

Gold - Does this correction make any sense?

The recent correction in gold has surprised some, as it is not behaving like the classic flight to safety seen in past periods of heightened tension and is instead trading more like a risk asset. We sense this correction is largely a liquidation of some excess speculation in the market and a broad based de-grossing of several winning trades over the past months that has pulled gold down irrespective of fundamentals. Most agree that the key drivers behind gold’s strength in recent quarters remain in place, including USD diversification, rising ETF demand, continued central bank buying and elevated geopolitical risks, but there is still limited conviction around what the new normal level should be. On Aura Minerals, there is absolutely nothing wrong with the investment case, with Era Dourada in Guatemala progressing well, the 2026 growth story on track and plenty of future growth optionality, but there is still a discussion around how high the P/NAV multiple can go. Some investors continue to argue that 1x P/NAV remains a ceiling for the stock rather than a floor.

Copper - Vulnerable given consensus trade

It was a good time to be on the road following our recent deep dive and initiation of Hudbay, Capstone and Ero Copper (link <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/118463> ). Given the hype around the copper story lately and elevated valuations, we sensed investors are looking to trim copper exposure given the downside risks to the global business cycle and some de-rating potential. We received some questions on potential sulfuric acid shortages and implications for LatAm, but these seem very modest risks. Southern Copper (rate Sell) remains a well perceived asset, but most agreed with our cautious stance on the equity story given very high EBITDA multiples in the range of 13-14x 2026, lack of relevant near term growth and some de-rating risk. On Hudbay, our top pick, we sense it’s still not on the radar for several investors who view it more as a Canada/US story than EM, but they were interested in our views on its cheap valuation at around 5x EBITDA, high FCF yields above 10% and growth potential.

Pulp - Cautious pricing and capital allocation

While we have been defending a Buy rating on Suzano for a while on the argument of very low expectations embedded in the stock, relatively faster deleveraging prospects, a de-rated multiple and excessive concerns on capital allocation, we sensed very little traction in the call. Several of the investors we met sold out of Suzano following the IP ordeal and never came back, with a key concern still being capital allocation and what the future EBITDA split would look like. Investors around here are quite bearish on pulp and believe the new long term floor is closer to US$550/t for hardwood China rather than US$600/t. We sense that greater visibility on future cash returns, with a more aggressive dividend policy, could bring attention back to Suzano, with most still preferring a less aggressive stance on future growth from the company. We observed very little interest in Klabin in our meetings.

Steel - Most are bearish and out of the space

With lingering concerns around Chinese exports and the flooding of global markets with cheap, unfairly priced steel, investors appear as bearish as ever on the sector. While there is some interest in Ternium and Gerdau’s US operations, there are questions around how sustainable a 50% tariff is in the region and how USMCA renegotiations could reshape steel flows and pricing in the region. Ternium is seen as the most interesting bottom-up story, with the EAF and DRI plant in Pesqueria ramping up into next year and supporting very strong FCF generation. On Gerdau, very depressed profitability in Brazil (crisis levels) and limited cash return potential remain the key overhangs. On Usiminas, there continues to be very low confidence in the path of EBITDA recovery given underinvested assets and fragile anti-dumping implications. On CSN, the key challenge remains understanding residual equity value in light of a massive and still unresolved debt situation.
