EMBRAER: Eve’s CFO comments on the eVTOL ramp-up

Last week, Money Times posted that Eve is advancing its urban air mobility strategy (see here <https://www.moneytimes.com.br/intensivao-de-testes-em-2026-e-entregas-em-2027-o-que-esperar-do-carro-voador-da-embraer-segundo-o-cfo-da-eve-air-mobility-lmrs/> ), focusing on flight testing its engineering prototype and progressing the type certification process with ANAC throughout 2026. Additionally, by 2027, the company plans to expand the testing campaign with six additional prototypes to support type certification and initial deliveries. In parallel, Eve is developing a portfolio of solutions and collaborating with public-private initiatives to advance the broader urban air mobility ecosystem. Lastly, it was flagged that key priorities through 2027 include deploying vertiports, adapting existing helipads with power-grid integration for recharging, defining operational procedures, and maturing urban air traffic management systems.

Our take: This development further supports the ongoing ramp-up of Embraer’s eVTOL project, following last year’s first flight and multiple subsequent test flights. We expect 2027 to be a key year focused on the certification campaign. Overall, these milestones serve as important derisking events for the project, and as progress continues toward initial deliveries, investors should increasingly attribute value to Embraer’s equity story. We therefore reiterate our BUY rating.

RANDONCORP & FRASLE: Soft February revenues

Randoncorp reported February/26 net revenues of R$996mn (–9% y/y and +8% m/m). Excluding Frasle, revenues reached R$587mn (–11% y/y and +5% m/m), indicating that core operations remain pressured, particularly in trailers, autoparts and selected industrial segments, despite some sequential improvement. Frasle Mobility posted net revenues of R$409mn (–6% y/y and +13% m/m), with the decline mainly reflecting temporary operational effects, including the learning curve from the ERP migration and the ramp-up of 4Mobility, which impacted productivity and volumes at the Extrema unit. Additionally, results were affected by a still-challenging consumption environment in Argentina and a tougher comparison base in the commercial segment, especially in Brazil and the U.S.

Our take: While February numbers still point to a weak y/y performance, the sequential improvement suggests a gradual normalization at both companies. At Frasle, the impact from ERP migration and logistics adjustments appears temporary and already moving toward stabilization, while underlying demand—particularly in the aftermarket—remains relatively resilient. At Randoncorp, the continued pressure ex-Frasle reinforces the still-soft cycle in trailers and autoparts, though the m/m recovery is a positive signal. Overall, we see the print as still weak but largely explained by transitory operational factors and a subdued industrial backdrop, with room for gradual improvement as these headwinds fade.

AEROSPACE: United cuts capacity; prepares for prolonged $100+ oil environment

United Airlines announced a reduction in flight capacity—targeting roughly ~5% of less profitable routes—as it responds to a sharp increase in jet fuel prices driven by geopolitical tensions in the Middle East. Management indicated that fuel costs have surged materially, prompting cuts focused on off-peak flights and select international routes. Importantly, the company is planning under a conservative scenario in which oil prices could reach up to ~$175/bbl and remain above $100/bbl through 2027, implying a potential multi-billion dollar increase in annual fuel expenses. Despite this, demand remains resilient, allowing airlines to partially offset cost pressures via higher fares and disciplined capacity adjustments.

Our Take: We view United’s decision as a rational capacity management response rather than a signal of structural demand deterioration. The key read-through for carriers under our coverage is the reaffirmation of fuel as the main short-term swing factor, with higher oil prices likely translating into a mix of (i) modest capacity discipline, (ii) fare increases, and (iii) some pressure on margins—particularly for carriers with limited hedging. That said, the still-solid demand backdrop and airlines’ improved financial discipline versus prior cycles should help mitigate downside risks. Overall, while the setup becomes more challenging at the margin, we do not see this as a demand-led downturn at this stage, but rather a cost-driven normalization environment.
