Industry View   Cautious

Erik W Woodring, Dylan Liu, Shawn Kim, Charlie Chan, Sharon Shih, Andy Meng, CFA & 5 more...	 
March 22, 2026

	
	 Our Global Smartphone Survey points to record 2026 upgrade rates, led by Apple vs. Android weakness, though memory headwinds are likely to dampen overall demand. We cut 2026 global shipments to -13% Y/Y and highlight favored vs least-favored smartphone stocks, preferring Apple over Android. 
Key takeaways	 

*	A constructive smartphone survey at first blush, with N12M upgrade rates in US and China hitting all-time highs, signaling faster replacement cycles ahead. 
*	However, Apple is only major brand positioned to benefit/gain share due to 1) new and advanced features, 2) better device quality, and 3) upgrade eligibility. 
*	Further, unprecedented memory inflation is set to push prices higher; lower brand loyalty & greater demand elasticity leave Android more vulnerable vs Apple. 
*	As a result, we are reducing CY26 smartphone shipments to 1.1B (from 1.3B), or -13% Y/Y, materially below prior, with Android -15% Y/Y and Apple -2% Y/Y. 
*	We also identify 16 stocks that will benefit from Apple outperformance vs. Android, as well as 10 "most challenged" names across Android OEM and supply chain. 

An unexpectedly positive smartphone survey at first glance, but largely a function of Apple strength offsetting Android weakness, with memory inflation likely to significantly dampen smartphone market demand prospects in 2026 and beyond. At first look, our late-2025 AlphaWise Global Smartphone Survey screens positively, with upgrade rates in the US and China — the world's two largest smartphone markets — expected to improve Y/Y and reach all-time survey highs in 2026, implying smartphone replacement cycles accelerate to a 10-year low (Exhibit 1 and Exhibit 2). However, the takeaways are more nuanced. Apple is the only major global smartphone vendor expected to gain share in 2026, as iPhone switching rates reach a 5-year high, suggesting Android vendors are unlikely to benefit from improving upgrade rates. Furthermore, we expect unprecedented memory cost inflation to negatively impact global smartphone demand in 2026 as device prices move notably higher — a dynamic not fully captured in our survey work — but for this to disproportionately impact Android vendors given lower brand loyalty and greater price elasticity of demand than iPhone. As a result, we are making two global calls with this report and our accompanying Greater China Smartphone report <https://ap-preview.webfarm.ms.com/eqr/article/webapp/9ac20138-fb44-11f0-8bb0-3a84fbe785bc> : (1) cutting our global smartphone shipment forecast to 1.1B units in CY26 and 1.14B units in CY27 (-13% Y/Y and +3% Y/Y respectively), down from 1.3B units (+3% Y/Y) in CY26 and 1.31B units (+1% Y/Y) in CY27, and (2) communicating a relative preference for Apple and Apple supply chain stocks over Android OEMs and their associated supply chains in 2026.

Global smartphone: A growing percentage of smartphone owners in the US (+3pts Y/Y) and China (+8pts Y/Y) are extremely likely to upgrade their devices over the next 12 months, resulting in a blended average upgrade intention of 43% (+5pts Y/Y), an all-time survey high, despite deteriorating upgrade intentions in EMEA

What did we learn from our Fall 2025 AlphaWise Global Smartphone Survey that influences our views? Four key takeaways:

1.	Global smartphone upgrade rates are expected to improve meaningfully Y/Y in 2026… Our 2025 AlphaWise Smartphone Survey indicates that US and China upgrade rates over the next 12-months are expected to expand 3 points Y/Y and 8 points Y/Y, respectively, with both markets reaching all-time survey highs. In contrast, upgrade rates in EMEA (i.e. the UK and Germany) are expected to deteriorate 3-6 points Y/Y. On a global blended basis, this implies 2026 smartphone upgrade rates improving by 5 points Y/Y to 43%, also an all-time survey high. This translates into a 0.3-year contraction in replacement cycles in 2026, the strongest since 2024.
2.	…however, upgrade strength is driven primarily by iPhone, with nearly all Android brands expected to lose share in 2026. This is evidenced by net switching rates — among the major smartphone brands surveyed, Apple remains the only brand with both a positive and improving Y/Y net switching rate. Apple's net switching rate improved by 5 points Y/Y to 11%, a 5-year high (Exhibit 3). All other major smartphone brands, with the exception of Google, exhibit negative net switching rates, implying that Apple is positioned to be the clearest market share beneficiary in 2026, while Android vendors are likely to lose share. Why? Among prospective US smartphone upgraders, advanced features and upgrade eligibility are the two reasons where Apple leads peers (Exhibit 4). Among prospective China smartphone brand switchers, better overall device quality is the #1 reason why smartphone owners in China are switching to the iPhone (Exhibit 5).
3.	Access to Generative AI features remains a top‑5 driver of smartphone upgrades. Access to GenAI features ranked #4 among global smartphone owners’ reasons for upgrading over the next 12 months, unchanged Y/Y (Exhibit 6). Together with rising stated importance of AI features in the US, the data suggests AI capabilities continue to influence upgrade decisions. However, iPhone owners’ perception of Apple Intelligence deteriorated Y/Y, with its ranking as an upgrade driver slipping from #5 last year to #7 this year (Exhibit 7). The US showed the largest deterioration, falling from #5 to #9, indicating Apple has yet to deliver the AI functionality that consumers expect and desire from their smartphone vendor.
4.	Outside of AI, smartphone upgrade drivers remain broadly stable Y/Y. The desire to own (1) a newly released smartphone model and (2) a device with more advanced features continue to rank as the #1 and #2 upgrade drivers this year. Camera capability gained importance Y/Y, while battery life slipped slightly, though both remain within the top‑5 drivers. New form factors (e.g., ultra‑thin designs) ranked as the #6 upgrade factor, lower than in prior years, likely reflecting trade-offs with camera and battery performance.

That said, what our survey doesn't fully capture is the impact that unprecedented memory inflation is likely to have on global smartphone demand, where we are turning increasingly cautious. Our AlphaWise Global Smartphone Survey was fielded from September to October 2025, before concerns about memory supply and cost inflation were well understood by consumers. Since then, multiple reports have surfaced of Asia-based smartphone OEMs nixing new launches (here <https://www.theverge.com/tech/895879/samsung-galaxy-z-trifold-discontinued-stock-sold-out?utm_source=chatgpt.com>  and here <https://www.techradar.com/pro/asus-will-no-longer-add-new-mobile-phone-models-in-the-future-ceo-confirms-no-more-zenfone-or-rog-smartphones-are-coming-as-it-pivots-to-focus-on-ai-products?utm_source=chatgpt.com> ), while key smartphone component suppliers have recently cautioned on the impact of memory cost inflation on demand (more here <https://www.barrons.com/articles/qualcomm-stock-price-arm-adr-65f673a2?utm_source=chatgpt.com> ). Put simply, memory cost inflation is likely to drive significant price hikes across all smartphone vendors, with lower-end OEMs bearing the brunt given much narrower margins than premium vendors such as Apple (Exhibit 8 and Exhibit 9). While we see demand elasticity as a global phenomenon and the key driver behind our decision to cut our CY26 smartphone shipment forecast to -13% Y/Y (from +3% Y/Y), we expect Apple to materially outperform Android, with iPhone shipments declining just 2% Y/Y in CY26 (impacted by new model launch timing) vs. Android shipments down 15% Y/Y.

Apple has higher gross margins for iPhones, leading to a lower impact to margins when memory costs are surging…

This magnitude of Android declines is supported by our latest supply chain checks, which point to a mid-teens Y/Y decline in Android smartphone shipments in CY26. Checks across multiple major component suppliers indicate 20%+ Y/Y shipment declines for most Android brands in 2026, with Samsung the notable exception, where sufficient memory supply supports single‑digit Y/Y growth (vs. MSe +5% Y/Y). Combined with our forecast for a 2% Y/Y decline in iPhone shipments in CY26, primarily reflecting a change in new iPhone model launch timing, with 50% of new models pushed to Spring 2027 from Fall 2026, we expect total 2026 smartphone shipments of 1.1B units, or ‑13% Y/Y (‑15% Y/Y for Android). These sharp unit declines largely reflect materially higher device pricing intended to partially offset surging memory costs, which is why our 2026 Android smartphone revenue forecast declines a more modest -5% Y/Y (from +9% Y/Y; Exhibit 10 and Exhibit 11).

On the back of this report, we are identifying 16 most-favored and 10 least-favored smartphone exposed stocks, with a clear preference for Apple over Android. In conjunction with our AlphaWise Global Smartphone survey, we highlight 16 EW or OW-rated stocks that we see as key beneficiaries of iPhone demand strength in 2026 vs. 10 EW or UW-rated stocks that we see as more challenged given their skew towards Android smartphone OEMs (Exhibit 12). Furthermore, alongside this report, our colleague Andy Meng is downgrading Sunny Optical (2382.HK) and Transsion (688036.SS) from OW to EW, and cutting estimates/price targets on over 10 smartphone-exposed stocks within his Greater China Technology Hardware coverage <https://ap-preview.webfarm.ms.com/eqr/article/webapp/9ac20138-fb44-11f0-8bb0-3a84fbe785bc> .

AlphaWise Survey Methodology: Online survey among ~2,000 18+ year old consumers per country in the U.S. and China, and ~1,500 per country in the U.K. and Germany, conducted during September 25th – October 22nd, 2025. The US, UK and German samples are representative of the general population in terms of age, gender and region in the respective countries. For China, the sample has expanded coverage since ’21 to include Tier 3 cities which are typically less developed than Tier 1 & 2 cities. We would note that the China sample skews younger, more affluent cohorts than the national population. The margin of error on the total sample is ±1.8% in the U.S./China and ±2.1% in the U.K./Germany at 90% confidence level; higher for subgroups. Comparable findings are trended to the Fall 2021-2024, and Spring 2021, 2020, 2017 and surveys.
