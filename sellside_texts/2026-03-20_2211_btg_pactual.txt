Mining concession draft proposal to face defeat 


Read the full document here >> <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/118844>  


Bicameral legislature anticipated to veto controversial measure


Peru’s Energy and Mines Commission has advanced a comprehensive overhaul of the General Mining Law, approving a substitute text by a margin of 11 votes to one, with three abstentions. This legislative package merges a dozen draft bills originating from diverse political factions and introduces three principal reforms: an escalation of concession fees, the imposition of more rigorous penalties, and a substantial reduction in the period allowed before a concession expires due to non-initiation of production. The amendments are particularly consequential for large-scale concessionaires, stipulating an increase in annual fees from US$3/ha to US$12/ha for holdings exceeding 100,000 ha – a threshold that encompasses nearly all major established mining firms. Moreover, the permissible window to commence extraction activities is halved from 30 to 15-years. The penalty framework is also restructured: following a decade of inactivity or failure to demonstrate minimum production or investment, concessionaires will incur a penalty equivalent to 10% of the annual concession payment. In addition, the new provisions mandate direct profit-sharing agreements with communities affected by mining operations, including the potential requirement for annual or periodic contributions to community trusts.

From a technical perspective, this legislative trajectory poses significant challenges for mining. The reduction of concession terms to 15-years is particularly problematic, echoing similar proposals from Mexico’s AMLO administration <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/93006>  in 2023, and disregards the protracted and capital-intensive nature of mineral exploration, resource delineation, and the subsequent transition to feasibility and bankability. 15-years is an inadequate timeframe to accommodate the full cycle of exploration, permitting, and development.

Additionally, the provision compelling mining companies to negotiate profit-sharing with local communities introduces substantial regulatory and operational complexity. Mining operations already generate fiscal contributions through existing tax and royalty structures; it is a core governmental function to administer these revenues and direct them toward social and infrastructure investment. The imposition of quasi-governmental responsibilities upon private operators – whether through mandated local allocations or compulsory profit-sharing – risks distorting the roles and expectations of both the state and the private sector. Given the lack of technical rigor underpinning these amendments, we assess that the measure is unlikely to achieve passage in Congress. In our view, its enactment would undermine the attractiveness of Peru’s mining sector, potentially setting an adverse precedent for parallel extractive industries such as O&G. 

We anticipate that the forthcoming bicameral Congress will subject the proposal to more robust scrutiny and predict that the current draft will be rejected – particularly as it appears to benefit informal miners. Politically, the issue is likely to feature prominently in the upcoming presidential campaigns, with candidates such as Lopez Aliaga – who has already advocated for shorter concession terms <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/115476>  – expected to leverage the debate as part of their electoral platforms.
