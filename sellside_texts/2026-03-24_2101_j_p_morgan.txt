Last week ,we hosted a call with Richard Shane, Head of J.P. Morgan's Consumer & Specialty Finance team (replay <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12848655&ad=4082682824&h=CVIGiue&sig=5feicqIt6GfmgaRjP0CdWTjOjaJ&v=2&isig=7QwzJUKrnwCGC0hPw4unToPgLAG&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/-lhpr9cl/KAL-1-Y56711HT/51c4f56f-f7dc-47d6-b7dc-57b40e261800> ). His outlook into 2026 remains cautious for credit card issuers given a softer macro backdrop and potential spillovers from the US‑Iran conflict. In particular, a sustained rise in oil prices could compress disposable income and hence spending. Key takeaways: (i) asset quality is mostly stable for now, though risks could build; (ii) scale remains critical for consumer finance profitability in the US; (iii) business models can differ, but for credit-oriented, some 75–80% of revenue coming from lending and 15–20% from transaction fees (MDR/interchange); and (iv) Rick highlights a low‑probability but high‑severity risk from potential rate caps or other regulatory changes. More details below. 

*	Outlook and sentiment into 2026. Rick started the year with a more cautious view on the sector, with stocks down ~15% YTD on average. For the first time in many years, he is not recommending any stock in the sector; all three major issuers have a Neutral rating (i.e. Capital One, American Express and Synchrony) . The more negative outlook is explained by higher inflation prints and cooling labor market. For more details, please see: Consumer Finance 4Q25 Preview <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12848655&ad=4082682824&h=sqc3ghJ&sig=6Jg5bjtUswLtIbQrvhJw0NvBSLk&v=2&isig=MbRTkSaamx3kuqygfEj1gi3409l&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/b6olpdla/GPS-5172565-0/51c4f56f-f7dc-47d6-b7dc-57b40e261800> (Jan 12, 2026). 
*	Macro risks remain with low visibility. Rick believes AI may impact the sector in the longer-term, but should not move the needle in 2026-2027. Rather, he sounded more concerned with the potential implications of the US-Iran conflict on oil prices and ultimately disposable. Specifically, higher gas prices should especially hurt lower- and middle-income consumers hardest, likely reducing spending and discretionary purchases. Ricks recalls that in 2022 oil prices moved up >40% in 3 months, driving a significant deterioration in the credit outlook.   
*	Asset quality & consumer behavior. When credit quality worsens, lenders tend to move upmarket, and conversely, move downmarket when the credit outlook improves. However, it remains unclear where we are in the credit cycle, as it is unclear how long these negative geopolitical factors will last. Only around 30-40% of outstanding debt is paid off monthly in the U.S., and a small segment pays in full, while most pay less. This is an ongoing issue in the US that differs from other markets. On the positive side, card payments are higher than before, as consumers want to maintain their credit cards as cash becomes less relevant, driven by the increased utility of electronic payments and declining cash usage. More people are paying off credit cards due to the need for electronic payment options, as many merchants have stopped accepting cash. The average American holds more than 4 credit cards, and credit card limits are increasingly viewed as savings by Americans. 
*	Industry dynamics favor scale. The credit card business is a scale business; many competitors have tried but failed to compete without sufficient scale. For most credit card lenders, 75-80% of revenue comes from lending vs 15-20%from transaction fees (MDR/interchange). Non-prime lending can be highly profitable in good cycles, but the US market has become less competitive post-GFC, with many institutions avoiding reputational risk. Regarding credit quality benchmarks, Rick mentioned issuers view a 680 FICO as the standard prime benchmark; 680–620 is near-prime, and below 620 is subprime. 
*	Regulatory and rate environment. Credit card interest rates are subject to state-level regulation, with domicile state laws applying nationwide, though proposed federal rate caps pose meaningful downside risk. Credit card interest rates are subject to 50 different state-level rate caps, but if issuers are domiciled in Delaware or South Dakota, the law allows them to practice the state's cap nationwide. Regulation has changed over time. Before 2010, issuers offered low fixed-rate cards but could change rates at any time; now, rates are variable and harder to change. Industry participants believe the recently proposed rate cap at 10% (for one year) would reduce supply and hurt the industry's penetration, especially for lower-end borrowers who pose a low-probability but high-severity risk. For more details, see: 10% Cap on Credit Card Interest Rates: Early Thoughts on the Impact Given All the Unknowns and Uncertainties <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12848655&ad=4082682824&h=MZugkoU&sig=AXEXQJ81P5Iz7qJzqxxWXQGZ4p4&v=2&isig=27RIdSAnao94sMX8NUUDBnqqqMJ&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/u780d7cf/GPS-5172997-0/51c4f56f-f7dc-47d6-b7dc-57b40e261800> . 
*	American Express (N-rated). The business model focuses on the higher-end segment, offering more resilience in a negative credit cycle but is not immune to the labor market. The recent weakness in the labor market (especially white-collar jobs) drove P/E multiples to decompress from ~22x to ~15x in 2-3 months. Customer behavior is more correlated with capital market performance, as high-income clients base spending decisions on their investment portfolios. This trend is not yet material but is worth monitoring given the market uncertainty. Unlike other issuers, their portfolio mix is less reliant on lending and has a higher mix of transactions and memberships, roughly ~20% lending, ~60% transactions (MDR/interchange), and ~20% annual membership fees. Transaction revenue is a high-ROE business, while lending is less profitable than competitors due to the typical AMEX customers paying off balances quickly. 
*	Capital One (N-rated). Capital One began offering subprime credit cards but then quickly shifted to higher end, and was only thing missing was the middle segment of the market, but was able to compete in this segment with the acquisition of Discover. It was initially focused on non-prime customers, but with strong underwriting, it was able to charge higher rates and achieve industry-leading risk adjusted margins. Historically, inorganic growth has helped drive expansion. Capital One has a lower ROTE compared to peers, but the business is more predictable. It has great management and continues to be founder-led. 
*	Synchrony (N-rated). Private label cards offer merchants cost advantages while providing consumers with targeted purchasing power, though acceptance is limited. Synchrony's private label cards work for most transactions but are limited in acceptance. Merchants favor them due to no merchant discount fees. Consumers see value in it as those cards allow spending without consuming credit limits.
