As the first interest rate cut takes effect in Brazil—with the Selic reduced by 25 bp to 14.75% on Wednesday—we review how Brazilian transportation stocks have behaved in prior cycles. Across the last four hiking and cutting cycles (2008/2009, 2011, 2015/2016, and 2022/2023), transportation sub‑segments outperformed the Ibovespa in the 6–12 months following the first Selic cut in three of them, with 2022 being the exception. For this cycle, we acknowledge that there may be a lower correlation with prior periods of outperformance, as easing begins against the backdrop of an ongoing war that is lifting oil prices—adding uncertainty to the depth and duration of cuts—while Brazil’s Presidential elections in 2H26 likely introduce another layer of volatility. The J.P. Morgan Economics team currently expects the Brazilian Central Bank to resume 50 bp cuts from the May meeting onward, taking the policy rate to 11.75% by year‑end (from 11.50% previously). *	Views on Previous Interest Rate Cycles. When analyzing the past four hiking/cutting cycles (2008/2009, 2011, 2015/2016, and 2022/2023), we observed that Transportation sub-segments—car rental, toll roads, and Infrastructure (ex-toll roads) & logistics—tended to outperform the most in the 6–12 months following the first Selic cut, with the exception of the 2022 cycle, during which our coverage underperformed. In the 2008/2009 cycle, the sector outperformed the Ibovespa by 55 p.p. on average 12 months after the first cut; by 27 p.p. in the 2011 cycle; and by 40 p.p. in the 2015/2016 cycle. However, in the most recent cycle, the transportation sector underperformed the IBOV by 17 p.p. in the 12 months after the initial cut. We attribute this primarily to bottom-up fundamentals. Moreover, despite policy easing, long‑term rates remained elevated, constraining a further re‑rating for long‑duration companies. Looking ahead to the current cycle, we believe the historical correlation may diverge, as stocks should also be influenced by two meaningful factors: the ongoing conflict in Iran, which is pushing oil prices higher, and Brazil’s presidential elections this year.
