ARGENTINA 

 	 

Softer Imports and Exports in February 

Bottom Line: The trade balance recorded a US$788mn surplus in February, larger than the US$275mn surplus recorded a year ago but down from the US$2,189mn surplus in the previous month.

Imports fell short of expectations for the fourth consecutive month, supporting the trade balance amid lower-than-expected exports in February. All in all, the cumulative trade balance over the last 12 months rose to a US$13.9bn surplus, approximately 2.0% of GDP, up from US$11.3bn (about 1.6% of GDP) by year-end 2025. Looking ahead, higher oil prices may boost Argentina's trade balance. The country is now a net oil exporter with a US$7.7bn energy surplus over the last twelve months.

DETAILS:

1. 

The trade balance recorded a US$788mn surplus in February, up from the US$275mn surplus recorded a year ago. The cumulative trade balance over the last 12 months is now at a US$13.9bn surplus, approximately 2.0% of GDP, up from the US$11.3bn surplus as of year-end 2025.

2. 

In February, the energy trade balance posted a US$486mn surplus, softer than the US$642mn surplus recorded in the previous year on the back of 27.6% lower energy exports (US$631mn vs US$872mn last year) as energy imports fell by 36.8% (US$145mn vs US$230mn last year).

3. 

Total trade flows (sum of exports and imports) declined by 7.2% yoy in February driven by lower exports which fell 2.9% yoy to US$5.96bn (below the US$6.82bn anticipated by economists surveyed by the central bank) and an 11.8% yoy decline in imports to US$5.17bn (similarly below the median estimate of economists surveyed by the BCRA at US$5.83bn).

4. 

The decline in exports was driven by a 7.1% yoy decline in quantities exported as export prices increased by 4.4% yoy. Exports of primary products rose by 8.2% yoy, but processed food exports declined by 10.1% yoy. Manufacturing exports, in turn, increased by 8.6% yoy and fuel and energy exports declined (-27.6% yoy). Sequentially, exports declined by 16.2% mom sa.

5. 

Lower imports, in turn, were driven by a 14.9% yoy decline in quantities as import prices rose by 3.7% yoy. The decline in imports was broad-based with capital goods imports down 7.6% yoy, and their respective parts and accessories declining 24.9% yoy. Fuel imports (-36.8% yoy), intermediate (-4.1% yoy) and consumption (-3.0% yoy) goods imports, as well as vehicle imports (-5.7% yoy), all declined from a year ago. Sequentially, imports increased 8.2% mom sa.

 	 

Data Today: 

Real GDP (4Q25); in line with the EMAE, monthly activity indicator, we forecast real GDP rose by 0.8% qoq sa in the fourth quarter of 2025. Our sequential forecast is consistent with activity rising 2.2% yoy in the quarter and 4.4% in all 2025.

SA

 	 

MEXICO 

 	 

Real GDP Firmed During 4Q2025 Driven by Domestic Demand; Negative Contribution from Net Exports 

Bottom Line: Real GDP growth accelerated to 0.86% qoq sa during 4Q2025, driven by robust household consumption (1.0% qoq sa), gross fixed investment (0.7% qoq sa) and also government consumption (0.8% qoq sa), mitigated by the negative contribution from net exports. The variation of inventories added a minor 10bp to quarterly growth, and the statistical discrepancy contributed with 1.8pt. On the supply side, the 4Q25 expansion of real activity was driven by the secondary (0.9% mom sa) and labor-intensive services (0.9% qoq sa) sectors. Within the secondary sector we highlight soft manufacturing (0.1% qoq sa, vs -1.2% qoq sa during 3Q) and the rebound in construction (+4.3% qoq sa, from -2.5% qoq sa in 3Q25) activity. Mining activity increased by 1.5% qoq sa, second positive print in ten quarters. The statistical carry-over for 2026 is at 0.8%. Overall, real GDP growth decelerated to 0.6% in 2025, from the already lackluster 1.4% in 2024, with household consumption up 1.1% and GFI down 6.3%. Despite higher US tariffs and lingering uncertainty exports performed well (7.0%) exceeding the annual growth of imports (3.1%).

Going forward, activity is expected to face headwinds from soft business confidence and peaking credit cycle. On the positive side, generous minimum wage increases, and a resilient labor market should offer some support to domestic demand. Investment is expected to continue to be impacted by both domestic and external uncertainty, in particular related to trade-policy direction in the US and the ongoing USMCA review.

DETAILS:

1. 

Real GDP grew 0.86% qoq sa during 4Q2025; a clear rebound from the 0.07% qoq sa Q3 variation. On an annual basis real GDP rose 1.8% yoy in 4Q2025, firming from -0.1% yoy 3Q25 print.

2. 

The economy grew a modest 0.6% in 2025, down from the lackluster 1.4% in 2024.

3. 

Final domestic demand firmed to +0.9% qoq sa in 4Q25, from 0.7% qoq sa in 3Q25. Private consumption grew a solid 1.0% qoq sa in 4Q25 (vs 1.3% qoq sa in 3Q25), government consumption 0.8% qoq sa, and gross fixed capital formation 0.7% qoq sa (vs -0.9% qoq sa in 3Q25), with private investment up 0.6% qoq sa (-0.5% qoq sa in 3Q25) and public investment up 2.0% qoq sa (-3.3% qoq sa in 3Q25).

4. 

Net exports' contribution to sequential growth was negative: -2.0pt, vs -1.9pt in Q3, given the underperformance of exports (+1.1% qoq sa) vis-à-vis the buoyancy of imports (+5.16% qoq sa). The variation of inventories added a minor 0.1pt neutral to 4Q sequential activity, and the statistical discrepancy added 1.8pt.

5. 

On the supply side, the 4Q25 expansion of real activity was driven by the secondary (0.9% qoq sa) and labor-intensive services (0.9% qoq sa) sectors, mitigated by the contraction of the low-weight primary sector (-1.4% qoq sa). Within the secondary sector we highlight soft manufacturing (0.1% qoq sa, vs -1.2% qoq sa during 3Q) and the rebound in construction (+4.3% qoq sa, from -2.5% qoq sa in 3Q25) activity. Mining activity increased by 1.5% qoq sa, second positive print in ten quarters.

6. 

On a seasonally and calendar adjusted basis, real GDP rose 1.8% yoy sa in 4Q25 (-0.1% yoy sa in 3Q25), with positive contributions from household consumption (4.0% yoy sa) and government consumption (1.4% yoy sa), mitigated by the decline of GFI (-3.9% yoy sa, vs -7.7% yoy sa in Q3). The contribution of net exports to annual growth during 4Q25 was negative and large: -3.8pt, given the large underperformance of exports (2.1% yoy) compared with imports (10.3% yoy).

7. 

Overall, real GDP growth decelerated to 0.6% in 2025 from the already lackluster 1.4% in 2024, with household consumption up 1.1% and GFI down 6.3%. Exports performed well (7.0%) exceeding the annual growth of imports (3.1%).

8. 

With the 4Q25 print, the statistical carry-over for 2026 is at 0.8%; that is, were the economy to remain flat during 2025 at the 4Q2025 level, real GDP would grow 0.8% in 2026.
