Our take: the announced reference price still implies a 30% discount to parity, but the price increase is still a decision of Petrobras. Yesterday, the federal government published Decree 12,883/2026 (here <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12833125&ad=4071871501&h=anH9AUO&sig=My2fa9vTzvGvjJTgKONGqzHjH5A&v=2&url=https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2023-2026/2026/decreto/D12883.htm> ) on the determination of the diesel reference price for subsidies in Brazil (see Diesel Relief Measures and 12% Export Tax <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12833125&ad=4071871501&h=5QOePKI&sig=NvhCfP9WIdDGAeDHpX4PEHpWiBR&v=2&isig=ExrIHRvCsLM3N6xI9Nm0e1g4GAT&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/ap0iil56/GPS-5233665-0/598504a5-cc1f-4124-98d4-c19aba483d33>  for additional context). The decree assigns to the Ministry of Mines and Energy the responsibility for setting the current reference price, with ANP defining the methodology for subsequent updates. It also establishes differentiated methodologies that, in Petrobras’s case, set the current R$3.70/l price as the cap for receiving the R$0.32/l subsidy announced last week. We calculate that, at this reference price, even factoring in the subsidy, the discount to import parity remains approximately 30%. We note that Petrobras operates under a formal pricing policy and governance framework. While external pressures may persist, we expect the company to continue making decisions consistent with these guidelines. 

	*	Decree 12,883 designates the MME to set the reference price and introduces differentiated methodologies for importers and producers. Decree 12,883 assigns the definition of the diesel reference price to the Ministry of Mines and Energy, with updates in subsequent periods to be determined by ANP. The decree also establishes distinct methodologies for importers and producers: (a) for importers and for producers refining imported crude or domestically sourced crude acquired from third parties, the reference price will follow import‑parity; and (b) for producers refining their own domestic crude, the reference price is set at the producer’s realization price at the time of the decree (approximately R$3.70/l in the case of Petrobras) plus R$0.32/l. 
	*	At the current reference price, the discount to parity would remain at 30% for refiners. We calculate that, at this reference price, even taking the subvention into account, the discount would marginally improve from 36% to 30%, but still remain strongly below parity. 
	*	The adoption of the new price reference is a choice, and investor focus remains on Petrobras’s governance. Historically, under the new policy, Petrobras has avoided rapid adjustments to short‑term volatility, prioritizing stable, predictable prices over immediately narrowing discrepancies with import parity. That said, after roughly three weeks of rising prices and a widening gap to parity, Petrobras announced a R$0.38/l diesel adjustment last week, underscoring its commitment to governance and its pricing framework. If the reference price remains as announced, we would expect Petrobras to decline the subsidy and maintain adherence to its established pricing policy.
