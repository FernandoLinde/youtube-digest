What to watch today - Rebuilding the house: Casas Bahia’s structural turnaround gains traction

Investor Day signals turnaround progress

We attended Casas Bahia’s Investor Day today, and the key takeaway is clear: the company has moved from survival mode to a recovery trend, with tangible operational and financial progress. Liability management was also front and center, reinforcing that balance sheet repair is not a one-off event but an ongoing strategic pillar. Management framed 2025 as an inflection year, with consistent improvements across growth, margins, and cash generation, despite a still challenging macro backdrop. The tone was notably more confident, shifting from defensive restructuring toward a more constructive outlook. While competition remains intense—particularly from generalist marketplaces—the company emphasized that it is no longer defined by past dislocations. Instead, Casas Bahia is increasingly positioning itself as a more disciplined, digitally integrated, and gradually a more sustainable platform, entering what management described as a “new moment” of acceleration.

Omnichannel consistency: Growth across formats validates strategy

One of the most important confirmations from the Investor Day was the consistency of growth across channels, reinforcing Casas Bahia’s omnichannel strategy. The company operates with a significant physical footprint—~2,000 stores, 24 distribution centers, and nearly 3 million sqm—serving a base of ~29 million active customers and ~R$45bn in GMV. Physical stores remain a core competitive advantage, particularly in categories such as appliances, where assisted sales and credit penetration are critical. Market share is particularly strong in the Southeast (~38–42%), while still underpenetrated in regions such as the North (~9%) South (~9%) and Mid-west (~17%), suggesting expansion opportunities. Importantly, stores also enable higher crediário (instalment plan) penetration, driving conversion and ticket size. At the same time, the company has increased its exposure to core categories (now 96% vs. 86% previously) and expanded its 3P marketplace participation, which is more capital-light given the absence of inventory risk. This balanced growth between physical and digital channels reinforces that Casas Bahia’s model is not only surviving but adapting to a more competitive retail landscape.

Balance sheet transformation – major deleveraging in latest quarters

The most impactful development discussed at the Investor Day was the structural transformation of Casas Bahia’s balance sheet, materially reshaping its financial trajectory. The company has executed a series of liability management initiatives, including debt conversions (~R$3bn), the launch of a crediário FIDC in January, and its first corporate market issuance via a supplier finance (risco sacado) FIDC. These initiatives are expected to generate ~R$2.8bn in financial expense savings over the next five years, driven by a meaningful reduction in funding costs. For example, crediário funding costs declined from ~150% of CDI to ~125%, while additional efficiencies were achieved in credit card prepayment (~1p.p.) and supplier financing (~4–5p.p.). Importantly, it has also extended its debt maturity profile, reducing near-term amortization pressure and improving liquidity visibility.

Credit and cash flow: potential advantages to reignite growth

Casas Bahia’s historical strength in consumer credit remains a central pillar of its business model, particularly in a macro environment that remains highly challenging. Categories such as appliances continue to depend heavily on installment financing, especially given constrained household liquidity and elevated interest rates. The company originated approximately R$10bn in crediário over the last 12 months, supported by improved underwriting and data analytics. The launch of the crediário FIDC further enhances funding efficiency and scalability, reinforcing credit as a competitive differentiator. At the same time, Casas Bahia has advanced several cash monetization initiatives, including the divestment of non-core assets (FIC), recovery of judicial deposits (DIFAL-related), monetization of tax credits, sale-leaseback transactions, and credit platform initiatives linked to brands such as Ponto Frio. Combined with tighter working capital management and cost discipline, these initiatives have significantly improved cash flow generation.

Operational transformation: from cash preservation to acceleration

Management outlined a clear three-phase transformation roadmap. The first phase (“cash is king”) focused on short-term survival, prioritizing cash generation, cost reductions, and operational simplification. The second phase emphasized selective investments, targeting projects with short payback periods and accelerating technology migration. The company has now entered, according to management, its third phase: acceleration. While the macro backdrop remains challenging, management is adopting a more constructive stance, making decisions aimed at unlocking growth rather than merely preserving capital. This includes revenue-focused initiatives alongside continued cost optimization, such as store rationalization and efficiency gains.

Strategy and positioning: focusing on core, scaling marketplace, unlocking value

Casas Bahia’s strategy is increasingly centered on focus and capital efficiency. By concentrating 96% of its exposure on core categories, the company differentiates itself from more diversified platforms with higher non-core exposure. At the same time, the expansion of its 3P marketplace improves capital efficiency and cash generation, as it reduces inventory intensity. Management also reiterated its ambition to become the largest 1P electronics retailer in Brazil, leveraging its scale, brand, and credit capabilities. Initiatives such as Google’s UCP integration are expected to further expand reach and competitiveness in digital channels. Finally, new value creation levers are emerging across three fronts: (i) capital structure optimization, reducing cost of capital and improving returns; (ii) debt profile optimization, with longer maturities and diversified creditors; and (iii) cash release and monetization initiatives.

All eyes on capital structure; we remain Neutral

In its most recent quarterly results, top line sustained an improving trend and SG&A efficiency gains, with welcome improvements in operating #s. That said, we still see a challenging ST outlook amid higher interest rates and a tough competitive landscape, which continues to weigh on the case, while the capital structure remains in the spotlight, sustaining our Neutral rating.
