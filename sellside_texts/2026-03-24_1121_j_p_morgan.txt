This monthly report provides a comprehensive summary of themes, top picks, and key valuation metrics for the airlines covered by J.P. Morgan worldwide. This product encompasses more than 30 companies broken down into the following regions: LatAm, North America, Europe, CEEMEA, and Asia.

*	Latin America (Guilherme Mendes): The short-term outlook for airlines in Latin America remains closely tied to the evolution of jet fuel prices, which account for nearly 40% of cash costs. Given the fluid nature of the current environment, we suggest caution – please refer to our joint report <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12833905&ad=4072413686&h=auypl7p&sig=6LyTBcGrFNh1A7AiKW70SkU041U&v=2&isig=N8QlnDgu1aVFYeRsyxdU2M1oqvx&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/jbobqvt5/GPS-5230955-0/56a71452-5e56-40a8-87e7-a62a06e73c47> with the Credit Research team, which includes a sensitivity analysis on jet fuel prices. For the companies under our coverage in the Latin America Airlines cluster, we favor LTM (OW) and CPA (OW) due to a combination of discounted valuation relative to historical levels, lighter balance sheets than peers, and consistent free cash flow generation and dividend payments. Both companies have consistently delivered robust operational results over the past few quarters. We recently initiated coverage on Aeromexico (OW), highlighting its positioning as the only full-service carrier in Mexico and leading carrier at Mexico City Airport (MEX), with higher exposure to international routes. However, we acknowledge that growth is likely to be more moderate than peers’, justifying a cheaper valuation. For VLRS (N), we acknowledge that the outlook for the aviation environment in Mexico should remain challenging in 2026. We view the proposed merger with Viva Aerobus as a positive development with upside potential, but expect any benefits to take time to materialize due to a lengthy approval process. Lastly, we upgraded Azul to Neutral (N) <https://jpmorgan.email.streetcontxt.net/platform/al?a=12833905&ad=4072413686&h=auypl7p&sig=6LyTBcGrFNh1A7AiKW70SkU041U&v=2&isig=N8QlnDgu1aVFYeRsyxdU2M1oqvx&url=https://markets.jpmorgan.com/research/email/scx/jbobqvt5/GPS-5230955-0/56a71452-5e56-40a8-87e7-a62a06e73c47>  following its emergence from Chapter 11, supported by a stronger balance sheet – with net debt/EBITDA expected at 2.4x in 2026. Pending equity dilution events should continue to pressure the stock in the near term, preventing us from taking a more bullish stance at this point. 

*	North America (Jamie Baker): We hosted CEOs from our entire North American airline coverage at our J.P. Morgan Industrials Conference last week. The message was consistent: industry-wide demand is off to the strongest start to a year ever, with multiple airlines seeing the highest sales days (and weeks) ever through mid-March. As for fuel – it’s too early to tell the impact and is dependent of the durability of the fuel spike, but we believe absent the fuel spike, every airline would have registered at the high end or better than previous guidance. Other key themes were the continued importance of brand-loyalty (through experience or card programs) to retain traffic, and corporate momentum is strong. Lastly, we believe that a high fuel price environment may accelerate much needed industry turnover at the lower cost of the spectrum thereby improving Legacy brand-loyal airlines outlooks 2027+. 
*	Mainland China, Hong Kong & Singapore (Karen Li): For Chinese airlines, the outlook remains complex and divergent, with persistent margin pressures from elevated operating costs given their lack of fuel hedging amid rising fuel costs attributed to the Middle East conflict. Furthermore, there remains fierce competition, constrained pricing power, and limited relief from anti‑involution measures. Geopolitical frictions (e.g., China–Japan; Thailand–Cambodia) and high‑speed rail competition continue to cap pricing flexibility, while industry cost pressures are likely to remain elevated into 2026. For non-China Airlines, we upgraded to Overweight SIA and Cathay on the back of robust demand, disciplined capacity management, and early balance sheet normalization, as both carriers are set to benefit from tight industry supply, strong Asia Pacific traffic growth, and their ability to flexibly redeploy capacity to attractive markets. We view the recent disruption in the Middle East as a near-term tailwind for demand rather than a structural shift, with both carriers positioned to benefit from elevated yields. That said, fuel  price remains the key swing factor, though hedges (SIA c.50% and CX c.30%) provide partial insulation. 
*	Japan (Ryota Himeno): Japan Airlines (JAL) announced its JAL Group Management Vision 2035 on March 2. JAL changed from its previous format of a five-year medium-term business plan combined with rolling plans to a 10-year vision with flexible single-year targets. It forecasts a larger-than-expected profit decline in FY2026. However, we take a positive view that JAL’s FY2030 EBIT target of ¥300 billion, supported by earnings improvement measures for domestic routes, is both higher than expected and a minimum commitment target. JAL’s shareholder return policy is unchanged, but it aims to substantially expand shareholder returns in conjunction with profit growth. 
*	Europe (Harry Gowers): For European airlines, we expect demand may fail to meet supply growth of +5%, which could translate into some pricing pressure this year. However, we forecast more manageable growth in long-haul at +4% vs. short-haul +6%. We are hence more cautious on European short-haul with higher risks around oversupply. Ryanair remains our top pick amongst the LCCs given more manageable and more mature supply growth, and we continue to view it as a structural winner given the lowest unit costs. We have an UW rating on easyJet, Neutral Wizz, and Neutral Jet2. For the Flag Carriers, we see potential for margin upside across the board in 2026 given improving Continental European GDP growth combined with an improving ex fuel unit cost picture. We forecast marginally positive European flag carrier pricing in 2026, with improving trends from a low point in Q3 25 and more favourable supply-demand dynamics. We are OW IAG, OW AF-KLM, and Neutral Lufthansa. 
*	Australia (JasonSteed):At the 1H26 results, QAN guided to domestic RASK of +3% and International RASK of +1-3%, at the lower end of our expectations, though still positive. Positive RASK will be important to offset a range of higher costs driven by elevated “Entry-Into-Service”, “Same Job, Same Pay” and fuel costs, which have risen since the start of the US/Iran conflict. While the company is ~90% hedged at the brent level for 1HCY26, crack spreads are unhedged. A US$1/bbl change in effective fuel cost (per barrel) impacts PBT by ~$50 million (~2%) for the full year. Lastly, we expect the proposed cap on interchange fees to have limited impact on long term loyalty EBIT targets at this stage, with a final RBA decision expected later this month. We maintain our Neutral on valuation. 
*	Turkey (Hanzade Kilickiran): Turkish Airlines’ MENA exposure is modest at about 6% of capacity, and we think it is relatively more protected as this capacity can be redeployed to West Asia and Africa where yields are typically higher and market share gains versus Gulf carriers are possible. Fuel hedging is modest at about 36% and is set around US$64 per barrel on a descending layer structure, which is more protective in the near term with up to 60% hedged next month and then tapering, so while this provides less protection than European peers and crack spreads have remained elevated, THY’s cost advantage and the typical three- to six-month lag to pass through fuel surcharges help mitigate exposure. Under an US$80 per barrel oil assumption, incremental fuel cost could be roughly ~US$50m per month, which is about 1% of total operating costs and is a rough estimate for a seasonally low quarter in terms of fuel consumption. 
*	India (Amyn Pirani): We believe IndiGo’s strong volume growth potential, combined with a potentially reducing cost curve, remains attractive over the long term. In the medium term, we continue to see the demand-supply dynamic as benign, particularly if global OEMs are constrained in deliveries. The ask is tougher in a cost inflationary environment, as IndiGo continues to face headwinds from both fuel and non-fuel costs, with limited scope to raise yields given tepid domestic air traffic and moderating international demand. In the near term, growth is expected to be further limited by moderation in supply, increased pressure on air fares, mix deterioration due to geopolitical developments, rising competition, and enhanced regulatory scrutiny against the backdrop of recent air accidents. Additionally, elevated jet fuel prices and continued INR weakness are likely to keep cost pressures high. We rate the stock Neutral and expect pressure on growth and margins.
