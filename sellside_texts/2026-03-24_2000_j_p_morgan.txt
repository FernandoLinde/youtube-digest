Metals Weekly 


We would like to flag a report from our global commodities analyst Gregory Shearer on the sharp sell-off in base metals this week as demand destruction risks from the ongoing energy disruption began to be priced in, and how this weighs against sizable but longer-dated supply risks.

Copper: Copper prices have pulled back ~11% month to date as demand destruction risks have begun to be priced in, though this decline is not yet close to fully discounting a more materially destructive macroeconomic backdrop. Notably, China’s micro fundamentals have improved, with onshore inventory drawing earlier than normal and China’s import arbitrage window opening. However, bearish macro risks should continue to dominate as long as energy prices remain elevated. From a technical perspective, the $11,100–11,200/mt zone is seen as likely medium-term support, with the $12,583–12,703/mt breakdown acting as major short-term resistance. If Brent oil stays at current levels ($110/bbl) for the remainder of the year, copper demand growth could be stripped by 1.4 percentage points, falling to only +1.2% global growth. 

Gold: Gold suffered a brutal 10% retreat this week as cracks in market complacency triggered a “sell everything” de-risking trade, compounded by momentum reversal, a break of the 50-day moving average, and rates markets flipping to price in the possibility of Fed rate hikes. However, this flush does not change the medium-term bullish outlook. The longer the energy disruption persists and the more sizable the growth impact becomes, the backdrop for gold is expected to flip materially bullish, amplified by a sharp shift toward Fed easing as the employment side of the dual mandate takes precedence. Technically, gold is expected to bottom above the $4,200–4,400 support zone, with the $4,967–4,996 tactical breakdown zone acting as initial resistance – a level the market is ultimately expected to rally through. 

Aluminum: Aluminum’s sharp sell-off this week came as a surprise given the more urgent supply dislocation risks facing the Middle East’s net exportable surplus of ~5 mmt (~7% of the global market). The absence of additional reports of supply curtailments beyond Alba’s March 15 announcement (~300 kmt/a, ~20% of capacity) left the metal exposed to the same demand-driven de-risking pressuring the broader complex. However, given the logistical infeasibility of re-routing the volumes of raw material imports and finished aluminum exports needed to sustain the GCC’s aluminum supply chain, further sizable capacity curtailments are expected in the coming weeks, turning a market dislocation into a more serious and prolonged supply outage. Aluminum has the highest demand beta to GDP among base metals (2.0x), meaning demand destruction could strip 2.2 percentage points from growth if oil stays at current levels, pushing total demand into contraction – but for now, supply risks should still bullishly outweigh eventual demand destruction potential. 

Nickel: Nickel faces the largest potential supply disruption among base metals, with an estimated ~12% of global supply at risk from sulphur constraints tied to the ongoing energy disruption. However, similar to copper, the buffer to supply disruption is modestly longer (1-3 months), meaning the first-order risk remains bearish as demand destruction is priced in the first. With base case 2026 demand growth currently estimated at 5.4%, the hit from elevated oil prices could be significant under a high-price, long-duration scenario, though the eventual accumulation of supply losses could set up a sharp V-shaped recovery.
