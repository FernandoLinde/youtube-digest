Keep an eye on 

 

Energy drives fertilizer: fertilizer drives food… Latest chart from Arend Kapteyn show the relationship between global fertilizer prices and global food prices. A simple regression suggests that energy prices explain about 46% of the variation in fertilizer prices. Fertilizer prices, in turn, explain close to 30% of the variation in global food prices. Applying CPI pass-through coefficients and noting that food weights are roughly twice energy weights, this points to an additional ~50bp of inflation in advanced economies and up to ~240bp in emerging markets, although with wide uncertainty. For comparison, the direct energy shock adds about 180bp. These are first-round effects only (weaker growth could dampen the impact), but absent moderation in energy prices, the food price shock in EM could rival the energy shock. Light (Sell, PT R$4) –Operational improvement overshadowed by balance sheet risk: Light reported 4Q25 adjusted EBITDA of R$418m, up from R$357m in 4Q24, driven mainly by temporary improvements in the distribution segment from lower non‑billed energy impacts and better provisions, while generation and commercialization were hurt by GSF effects. Brazilian Banks – Merchant satisfaction improving, fintech substitution risk remains low: UBS Evidence Lab’s Jan‑26 survey of 462 merchants shows overall satisfaction with primary banks rising to 81% (vs. 80% in 2024), with Itaú leading at 83% satisfaction, followed by Bradesco at 75%. Itaú also expanded its merchant share by +6pp to 25%, widening the gap versus Bradesco (14%), while Santander Brasil and Caixa each held 9%, indicating renewed momentum for large incumbents. Willingness to switch the main bank to a fintech or acquirer‑led banking solution remains limited, as 60% of merchants would not replace their main bank (flat YoY), reflecting strong satisfaction and broader product offerings from banks. Credit penetration remains low, with only 36% of merchants holding bank loans (vs. 34% in 2024), despite 80% having received credit offers; notably, 47% of those offered credit by acquirers viewed the terms as worse than banks’. Overall, the findings support a positive stance on Brazilian banks, with Buy ratings on Itaúsa, Santander Brasil, Bradesco and Nubank. Overall, Tasso Vasconcellos sees data as supportive for his expectations of stronger output growth over the year, with UBSe and most consensus forecasting production around ~2.60Mbpd for 2026E, though he notes new units' lack of major progress in ramp-up during the month as something to monitor. Chile FI/FX Strategy – CLP vulnerable to oil volatility, USDCLP 1,000 in sight: The Chilean peso remains one of the most exposed EM currencies as oil-driven terms‑of‑trade headwinds, low carry and weak H1 seasonal USD flows persist into Q2, with limited FX valuation support despite the recent reset toward ~910. Chile’s energy trade deficit (~3% of GDP) could offset most 2025 gains from copper exports if oil stays elevated, and under a more adverse scenario (oil at $100/bbl) the current account deficit could widen to ~4% of GDP. FX buffers are thin and the central bank is unlikely to lean meaningfully against weakness, while post‑election dynamics historically point to ~4% CLP underperformance in the months following inauguration. Against this backdrop, USDCLP could rise another ~5% before valuations look stretched, with the 1,000 level reachable if energy prices remain high or copper corrects. On rates, near‑term inflation repricing (~50bp) contrasts with soft core inflation (~2.5% y/y) and rising growth risks, suggesting limited scope for hikes and potential for rates to drift toward ~4% later in the year if growth concerns dominate. US Economics – Funds rates risk and uncertainty: Jonathan Pingle sees an increased widening of potential Fed outcomes. While the risks of rate hikes might be rising, the risk of a lot of rate cuts is rising too. He thinks the risk to the outlook is not just energy prices and supply disruptions. Consumer spending is such a large contribution to growth, and continues to grow faster than income, equity market valuations softening are also a risk. He would not put zero weight on the 1990 experience, or assume recession odds are arbitrarily low now. As much as speculation over potential rate hikes has risen, risks to growth and the labor market have risen too. Based on the move in Energy: he revised up headline inflation in 2026. Relative to before the oil price moves, he has now added 50 bp of headline inflation, lowered 2026 Q4/Q4 GDP growth by 20 bp, and revised up the year-end unemployment rate a tenth to 4.6%. Global Equity Strategy – Select defensives and luxury as risk asymmetry rises: UBS argues for a barbell positioning given binary Middle East risks and a macro backdrop that increasingly disfavors cyclicals. Defensives are attractive as staples and healthcare have underperformed since 27/2, yield curves continue to flatten (historically negative for cyclicals), and cyclicals (ex‑tech/financials) look expensive on P/E and P/B while not pricing weaker PMIs or earnings downgrades; UBS expects PMIs to fall and GDP forecasts to be revised down, with oil risks requiring growth downgrades to balance supply/demand. Several defensive pockets screen abnormally cheap, including household products, flavors, healthcare equipment, pharma, UK food retail, and utilities, which benefit from energy security, renewables incentives, AI‑driven T&D growth, and reasonable valuations; preferred names include Reckitt, P&G, Novonesis, Symrise, Abbott, Alcon, AstraZeneca, Roche, Enel, NRG. Luxury complements the barbell as it is 1 std dev cheap vs the market, historically resilient after deep sell‑offs, with EPS back to trend, capex below trend, improving revisions, support from a weaker euro, and limited disruption risk; focus on LVMH and Richemont.
