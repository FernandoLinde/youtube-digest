BRAZIL 

 	 

Copom Minutes: Likely to Reiterate Cautious Rate Setting/Calibration Approach Amidst High External Uncertainty 

The central bank will publish on Tuesday (March 24) the minutes from the March 18 Copom meeting. The Copom initiated the rate normalization cycle with a cautious 25bp rate cut, driving the Selic to 14.75% (unanimous decision). We expect the minutes to reinforce the cautious approach to near-term policy calibration shown in the post-meeting statement given high uncertainty around the depth and duration of the conflict in the Middle East and its impact on the key drivers of inflation.

In the minutes we will be looking for:

1. 

Discussions around the framework the central bank is using to analyze and react to the spike in oil prices and rising global geopolitical uncertainty.

2. 

The set of conditions that would lead the central bank to accelerate, maintain, or altogether interrupt the easing cycle.

3. 

Discussion about the policy room for additional cuts amidst high uncertainty, rising inflation expectations, and inflation forecast above the 3.0% target over the entire relevant horizon for monetary policy.

4. 

Discussion on the real business cycle and the expected evolution of the output gap.

5. 

Updated characterization of labor and credit market conditions.

6. 

Discussion on the short- and medium-term inflation dynamics and risk around it.

At the March 18 meeting the Copom did not provide explicit Forward Guidance. Instead affirmed that amidst the significant increase in uncertainty, “serenity and caution in the conduction of monetary policy” is required, so that the next steps in the policy rate calibration could incorporate new information about the depth and duration of the conflict in the Middle East, as well as the direct and indirect impact on the price level through time.

The balance of risks for inflation did not change (surprisingly), with both upside and downside risks characterized as higher than usual but having intensified after the start of the conflict in the Middle East.

1. 

As upside risks to the inflation outlook and inflation expectations, the Copom repeats: (i) a longer period of unanchored inflation expectations; (ii) stronger-than-expected resilience of services inflation due to a more positive output gap; and (iii) a combination of domestic and external economic policies that generate a higher-than-expected impact on inflation through, for example, a persistently more depreciated currency.

2. 

As downside risks, the Copom repeats: (i) a deeper deceleration of domestic economic activity; (ii) a deeper deceleration of global activity driven by the trade-shock and a backdrop of higher uncertainty, and (iii) a disinflationary decline in commodity prices.

 	 

Monetary Policy Report (MPR): Convergence of Inflation to Around the Target Likely Only By Late 2027. 

The central bank will publish on Thursday, March 26, the 1Q26 Monetary Policy Report (MPR). In the 1Q26 MPR, the central bank will unveil updated forecasts for real GDP growth, provide conditional forecasts for inflation through 3Q2028 and discuss the domestic and external balance of risks for both growth and inflation.

The real GDP growth forecast for 2026 is expected to be modestly revised upward from the latest 1.6%, which would suggest a milder narrowing of the output gap. Furthermore, we expect the MPR to present conditional projected inflation paths in line with those of the March 19 Copom post-meeting policy statement: headline inflation forecasted for 2026/3Q27 at 3.9%/3.3% (both above the 3.0% target).

In the QIR we will be looking in particular for updated estimates and projections for the output gap (projected to shift from 0.5%/0.2% in 3Q/4Q 25 to -0.4% of GDP by 2Q27 in the Dec MPR), and the neutral real rate (r*; currently at 5.00%) since that may condition the depth of the easing cycle. We will also be looking for the inflation forecasts for 4Q27, as they will roll into the relevant horizon during the next two meetings. These forecasts will provide hints on the timing for the beginning of rate normalization cycle.

CHILE 

 	 

Central Bank to Incorporate Oil Shock into a Revised Baseline Scenario 

On Tuesday, March 24, the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) will make its monetary policy decision, and on Wednesday, March 25, the central bank will publish its quarterly Monetary Policy Report (IPoM). With oil prices rising sharply due to the geopolitical conflict in the Middle East, we expect the MPC to take a more conservative wait-and-see stance, with a stronger emphasis on risk management considerations. As a result, despite the dovish guidance provided at the January monetary policy meeting and two favorable inflation prints in January and February, we expect the central bank to keep the policy rate unchanged at 4.5%, 25 bps above its point estimate of the neutral rate.

The national accounts data for the fourth quarter of 2025 showed that final domestic demand (both consumption and investment) stayed dynamic and resilient towards year-end. Growth for 2025 ended 0.1pp above what BCCh had anticipated in the December IPoM (2.5% vs. 2.4% expected). Growth for 2023 and 2024 was revised up by 0.2pp each to 0.7% and 2.8%, respectively. In our assessment, the resilient activity momentum, along with improving business and consumer sentiment indicators and a constructive outlook for investment in large projects, makes a more accommodative (or slightly less restrictive) monetary stance less pressing.

In the central scenario incorporated in its December IPoM, BCCh had projected that the average Brent-WTI oil price would track at US$62 per barrel in 2026. This forecast will require a substantial upward revision in the March IPoM. The GS Commodities Research team has revised its 2026 Brent-WTI forecast higher from US$54 per barrel at the start of the year to US$82 under a base-case of heavy disruption of oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz through April 10. In our view, the size of the shock and the uncertainty around how long it will last argue in favor of acting conservatively and pausing BCCh’s easing cycle. Global financial conditions have also tightened (the December IPoM assumed two Fed cuts in 2026), and the exchange rate has depreciated (the last IPoM incorporated an appreciation of the real exchange rate through 2026) which could lead to the price shock broadening beyond fuel prices into other tradable goods.

We recently revised up our year-end inflation forecast from 2.8% to 3.5%, above the 3.2% the central bank projected in the December IPoM and downgraded our growth forecast to 2.3% from 2.5% before, below the 2.5% midpoint of the 2.0% to 3.0% range in the last IPoM. Uncertainty around our forecast has increased, and domestic policy decisions could also affect the magnitude and persistence of the oil-price shock on inflation. We highlight that Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz has emphasized in recent days potential changes to the fuel price stabilization mechanism (MEPCO) that could entail a faster adjustment of domestic fuel prices. In our understanding, however, increases above the current three-week 2.4% adjustment cap would require congressional approval and are unlikely to be incorporated into the March IPoM baseline, even if announced earlier in the week. We also note that inflation expectations remained anchored in the central bank’s latest survey of economists (EEE) conducted earlier this month (although they typically update with a lag and the right tail increased) but rose in last week’s survey of traders (EOF).

All in all, we expect both the MPC’s post-meeting statement and the March IPoM to emphasize a message of caution amid elevated uncertainty. The MPC will likely acknowledge a higher but transitory inflation path, especially in non-core inflation. We also expect BCCh to stress that while higher oil prices will push inflation up in the near term, the shock could turn to be disinflationary over the medium term if activity slows and the external impulse weakens. In fact, copper prices have also declined in recent days, which, if persistent, could amplify the negative impact on Chile’s terms of trade.

MEXICO 

 	 

MPC Meeting: Should I Stay or Should I Go? 

The central bank Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) will announce its rate decision on March 26 at 3:00pm EST. At the Feb 5 meeting the forward guidance turned clearly dovish, hinting that a rate cut would be considered at the March meeting (“looking ahead, the MPC will evaluate enacting additional rate cuts”). In the meantime, the central bank baseline scenario was upended by the conflict in the Middle East which led to a sharp increase in oil prices, strong USD, bear flattening of the US curve (with higher real rates reflecting a hawkish policy shock), rising risk-version, and tighter financial conditions. All amidst heightened global geopolitical risk.

A Hawkish Cut. We are leaning (barely so) towards a 25bp rate cut, to 6.75%, given the consistently dovish guidance and Banxico-speak, just mild MXN depreciation since the last meeting, significant tightening of domestic financial conditions, softening outlook for US and global demand, and the fact that, so far, the impact of the early excise tax and import tariff hikes seems to have been contained. Furthermore, the majority of the MPC directors do not seem overly concerned with the current inflation pressures and above-target inflation, choosing to focus instead on the soft growth outlook (large negative output gap) and the contribution to the inflation dynamics of a strong MXN. In addition, the risk of a major spike in fuel prices in the short-term is contained by the fact that the govt reached a voluntary 6-month agreement with gas stations to cap gasoline prices and has lowered the IEPS excise tax on fuel sales.

In case of a cut, we anticipate a split decision (with director Jonathan Heath voting for a hold) and the central bank raising the bar for another cut in May (hawkish cut).

Our degree of conviction that the central bank will cut is not high. In fact, we assess a high 40% probability of no cut in a nod to the ongoing oil price supply shock and heightened uncertainty. If the MXN/USD exchange rate keeps depreciating this week and Tuesday's first-half March core CPI is much higher than expected, the likelihood of holding a 7.00% rate will increase, eventually becoming the most probable outcome.

Since the last meeting MXN/USD weakened (to 17.82 from around 17.33 ahead of the Feb 5 meeting), oil prices (Brent) are up approximately 56% (Brent US$105.2barrel), and 1- and 10-year UST rates moved by up by 39bp/+19bp to 3.80%/4.37%, and 5-year CDS spreads widened by 21bp to 108. Headline and core inflation expectations for 2026 deteriorated (+5/+6bp to 4.00%/4.17%) and the median expectation for growth in 20206 rose by 20bp to 1.50%.

The inflation backdrop remains challenging. Annual headline inflation is now tracking at 4.13%, above the upper-limit of the inflation target band. Tax and import tariff hikes pressured core-food in Jan, with the impact tapering during Feb. The normalization of non-core inflation from very low prints (perishable food prices in particular) is now driving headline inflation up; inflation among freely-determined prices accelerated to 4.60% yoy, with core inflation tracking at a high 4.48% yoy. In addition, high labor costs are likely to keep services inflation high in the 4%-handle for a while. Finally, we highlight that the recent oil price shock and MXN depreciation is a new headwind to inflation, which depending on the duration and intensity of the shock may prompt the central bank to adopt a more near-term conservative stance.

Real GDP growth accelerated to 0.86% qoq sa during 4Q2025, driven by robust balanced contributions from household consumption (1.0% qoq sa), gross fixed investment (0.7% qoq sa) and government consumption (0.8% qoq sa), mitigated by the negative contribution from net exports. On the supply side, the 4Q25 expansion of real activity was driven by the secondary (0.9% mom sa) and labor-intensive services (0.9% qoq sa) sectors. Within the secondary sector we highlight soft manufacturing (0.1% qoq sa, vs -1.2% qoq sa during 3Q) and the rebound in construction (+4.3% qoq sa, from -2.5% qoq sa in 3Q25) activity. Overall, the economy grew a modest 0.6% in 2025, decelerating from a soft 1.4% expansion in 2024. Bank credit growth decelerated by 70bp to 1.9% yoy in real terms in Jan, with nominal growth down by 60bp to 5.8% yoy. Credit to households’ ex-mortgages expanded 0.6% mom sa but continues to decelerate on an annual basis. Corporate credit growth was flat sequential in Jan (-0.4% mom sa in Dec) and the annual growth rate dipped into negative territory (-0.6% yoy) in real terms. The labor market backdrop remained resilient: Seasonally adjusted, the unemployment rate rose a minor 2bp in Jan to a tight 2.62% (just +14bp off the Oct-24 record low). The Feb Manufacturing PMI was stable in Feb (still tracking slightly contractionary territory) while the Non-manufacturing PMI firmed but is still tracking below the 50 threshold. Consumer and business confidence firmed.

 	 

Firm Jan Retail Sales and Non-Financial Services Activity 

Bottom-Line: Retail sales recorded a solid 1.0% mom sa in Jan, above consensus for a milder 0.2% increase. Non-financial services sequential activity rose 0.3% mom sa, partially offsetting the Dec decline. The retail sales carry for 1Q2026 is tracking at 1.4% qoq sa, and for services at 0% qoq sa. Sequential real wage growth was positive in both retail (1.9% mom sa; 7.0% yoy sa) and services (0.4% mom sa; 2.6% yoy sa), with employment growth firmer in retail (0.4% mom sa; 1.3% yoy sa) and weak in services (-0.1% mom sa; -1.1% yoy sa).

Going forward, household consumption of goods and services is likely to continue to face headwinds from lingering domestic and external uncertainty. On the positive side, household spending is likely to benefit from generous minimum wage increases, a still resilient labor market backdrop, and solid though moderating real wage growth.
