FLRY and PSSA now together discussing a potential deal with ONCO

This morning, Fleury (FLRY) published a material fact regarding a non-binding term sheet signed with Porto Seguro (PSSA) and Oncoclínicas (ONCO). The agreement is basically the same deal (still under negotiation) between Porto and Oncoclínicas that was disclosed one week ago. In other words, Fleury is just joining the same non-binding agreement. ONCO is committed to negotiating exclusively with Porto and Fleury for a period of 30 days (starting on March 13th, which was the day the deal was originally signed between PSSA and ONCO). Most importantly, Porto and Fleury will still conduct formal due diligence on Oncoclínicas before potentially moving forward with the deal.

Some preliminary details on the NewCo

The transaction would involve the creation of a NewCo that would consolidate all assets and operations related to ONCO’s oncology clinics. In this structure, Oncoclínicas would contribute these assets to the NewCo in exchange for shares, while the NewCo would also assume a certain amount of liabilities currently held at Oncoclínicas (debt, M&A payables, tax liabilities, among others), limited to a maximum amount of R$2.5bn. We note that ONCO’s net debt stands at R$2.8bn (as of 3Q25, pro forma for the recent capital increase). According to ONCO’s material fact (published a few days ago), hospital operations would remain outside the NewCo.

ONCO and FLRY would inject capital into the NewCo

Porto and Fleury, in turn, would inject R$500mn in equity (through a new controlling block – HoldCo), subscribing to ordinary shares that would grant them control of the voting capital of the NewCo. The equity stakes in the HoldCo are still to be defined between Porto and Fleury. In addition, the NewCo would issue R$500mn in convertible debentures, with a four-year term and remuneration of 110% of CDI until conversion. These debentures would be subscribed by the HoldCo (Porto and/or Fleury), while ONCO would have the right to subscribe for up to 30% of the total debenture amount.

We still have some questions about the structure

The material fact regarding this potential deal still lacks several important details for a proper assessment of the structure. At first glance, however, we see the transaction as somewhat challenging. In light of the ongoing discussions related to ONCO’s high indebtedness (such as waivers and a potential out-of-court restructuring process), if approved, this structure could allow the company to continue operating, which appears to be what Porto aims to ensure with this deal. However, even assuming some debt haircut, many stakeholders still have doubts about the company’s organic deleveraging trajectory. As of 3Q25, ONCO reported net leverage of ~4.2x (or ~3.0x pro forma for the full capital increase), while current debt stood at roughly R$2.8bn.

Hard to understand move by Fleury; more questions than answers

Of course, the deal would bring significant scale to Fleury’s oncology business, as Oncoclínicas has more than 140 clinics. However, at first glance, we have some difficulty understanding Fleury’s strategic rationale for this transaction. The company already has some exposure to the oncology segment through its joint venture with Bradesco and Beneficência Portuguesa (Croma Oncologia) and, in our view, would have the capacity to scale this business organically. Assuming a R$500mn cash outlay by Fleury (equity in the NewCo plus convertible debentures on a pro rata basis, assuming a 50/50 split with Porto), this would represent 6% of Fleury’s market cap. Therefore, it is not clear at this stage what incremental benefits Fleury would gain from participating in this new structure, especially when considering the potential risks involved. On the other hand, we can see a more evident rationale for Porto, given the increasing relevance of Oncoclínicas in its medical claims over time. Porto may simply be looking to ensure the sustainability of a provider that has become important to its network.

Changing FLRY’s investment case? We remain Neutral on ONCO

Although some details still need to be clarified, at first glance, if this non-binding agreement evolves into a binding one, we would initially view it as negative for Fleury’s investment case. In our view, the company has a relatively stable business model, largely characterized as a dividend and free cash flow yield story, and we believe this transaction could begin to shift that perception, particularly as it would co-control a quite leverage new company under a potentially riskier structure. Regarding Oncoclínicas, a potential agreement with Porto and Fleury could provide additional financial support and help extend the company’s liquidity runway, which could ultimately be viewed as a positive from an equity perspective. That said, given the significant uncertainties surrounding the investment case and its stretched balance sheet, we remain Neutral on ONCO.
