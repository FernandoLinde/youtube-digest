Today, we hosted Mr. Carlos Aguiar (Head of Sales ) and Mr. Filipi Campos (Head of Market Insights) from Close-Up International in Brazil. The main takeaways from the call were: 1) the Brazilian pharma market remains structurally robust, growing double-digits over the past decade, with large chains representing ~50% of sales; 2) GLP-1 products should continue to print solid growth trends, with the expiration of the patent for semaglutide (today in Brazil) being a relevant trigger to significantly increase the TAM; and 3) Tirzepatide (Mounjaro) represents today ~65% of GLP-1 sales but its growth is constrained by supply. Please find the detailed notes from the webinar below. The presentation used by Close-Up International can be provided upon request. 

*	Bottom Line. Brazilian pharma retail remains structurally robust (~US$48bn market, double-digit growth), but GLP-1 therapies have become the central driver of incremental value creation. With ~4.7% of market value and growing rapidly, the category is expanding the market, reshaping prescription behavior, and increasing channel concentration. Supply constraints, high pricing, and limited distribution suggest the market is still underpenetrated. The next phase should be driven by broader access, increased competition, and price normalization, supporting continued strong growth while gradually expanding beyond the current high-income patient base. 
*	Brazil Pharma Retail: Category Structure and Demand Profile. The Brazilian pharma retail market figures among the top globally, and has delivered consistent low double-digit value growth over the past decade. This growth is notably price/mix-driven, with unit expansion running at only ~4–5% p.a., implying strong premiumization. The market remains heavily skewed toward branded products, with prescription drugs accounting for ~50% of total value (rising to >55% when including GLP-1s), non-medication categories at ~30%, and OTC at ~14%. The out-of-pocket nature of the system continues to shape purchasing behavior, reinforcing brand preference but also embedding price sensitivity at the margin. 
*	Channel Concentration and Route-to-Market Dynamics. Brazil has a highly fragmented physical footprint (~100k pharmacies), but revenue is concentrated: large chains account for ~48% of total sales while representing only ~15% of stores. This concentration drives superior sales productivity and makes these chains the primary access point for premium therapies, such as GLP-1. Independent pharmacies still represent >50k stores, but lack the infrastructure for higher-complexity products. Meanwhile, digital penetration has accelerated meaningfully post-COVID, reaching up to ~20% of sales for certain players, signaling a structural shift in fulfillment and consumer access channels. 
*	Local Pharma Strength and Competitive Positioning. Local companies dominate the competitive landscape, accounting for 4 of the top 5 players and 6 of the top 10 in Brazil. Leading domestic players generate US$2.5–4.0bn in annual revenues and are growing at mid-to-high teens in key segments (e.g., ~16% for some large players), outpacing multinationals in several cases. Their strength is most evident in branded generics (~37% of the prescription market) and generics (~30%), but they are also expanding into exclusive products (~23%), leveraging local manufacturing and distribution capabilities. This positions domestic players as structurally advantaged in scaling new categories and responding quickly to demand shifts. 
*	Obesity as a Major Long-Term Demand Driver. Brazil presents one of the largest addressable obesity markets globally, with ~39 million obese adults (BMI >30) and >60% of the adult population classified as overweight (BMI >25). The condition is strongly associated with comorbidities: ~30% of obese individuals present cardiovascular diseases, alongside high incidence of respiratory disorders, cancer, diabetes, and renal conditions. This reinforces obesity as a central node in chronic disease management and a key driver of long-term pharma demand. 
*	GLP-1s as the Main Force Reshaping the Market. GLP-1 therapies have rapidly scaled to ~4.7% of total pharma retail value (vs. ~2.2% in 2023), effectively doubling their share in a short period of time. Despite representing only ~0.1% of total units sold, the category’s high price point drives outsized value contribution. Within prescription drugs (~US$27bn), GLP-1s already represent ~8.5% of value, highlighting a strong skew toward premium therapies. Importantly, GLP-1s have been a key contributor to overall market growth (~11–12% recently), indicating that they are expanding the total addressable market rather than merely redistributing existing spend. 
*	Tirzepatide’s Rapid Disruption of the Competitive Landscape. Tirzepatide has scaled exceptionally fast, reaching ~65–66% market share in value within less than 12 months of launch. In its first month alone, it captured ~40% of category sales, and has since maintained leadership while expanding the total market. The product generated ~US$1bn in sales in under a year, making it one of the most impactful launches in Brazilian pharma history. Adoption has been supported by superior clinical efficacy and rapid physician uptake, with prescription share exceeding ~55% and a similarly dominant position in prescriber penetration. 
*	Semaglutide: Still Growing, but Losing Share. Semaglutide remains a large and growing market, with ~35% y/y expansion in LTM terms, but has seen a sharp decline in market share. It moved from ~100% of the category in early 2025 to ~33% by early 2026. Within individual products, leading SKUs declined from ~70%+ share to ~20–25% ranges over the same period. Despite this, absolute sales continue to increase, supported by overall category expansion. Licensing strategies have allowed partial share stabilization, with licensed branded generics already reaching ~5% share within months of launch. 
*	Liraglutide as a Fading First-Generation Segment. Liraglutide has entered structural decline, with the molecule contracting ~60% y/y in value and volume. While lower-priced alternatives exist, including branded generics, adoption remains constrained by inferior efficacy and the need for daily administration versus weekly injections for semaglutide and tirzepatide therapies. Even where initial uptake of generics was strong (e.g., ~50% share shortly after launch), supply constraints and shifting patient preference have limited sustained growth, reinforcing the migration toward second- and third-generation molecules. 
*	Supply Constraints as a Key Determinant of Near-Term Growth. Supply remains a key bottleneck, with distribution concentrated in large chains and incomplete coverage across the ~100k pharmacy universe. Cold-chain requirements further restrict availability, particularly among independent pharmacies. Evidence suggests that even leading products are not fully distributed across all potential points of sale. As a result, current penetration likely understates true demand. Incremental supply—via local manufacturing, partnerships, and new entrants—should unlock additional growth, particularly as availability expands beyond the ~48% of sales currently concentrated in large chains. 
*	Regulation Changed the GLP-1 Selling Process, but Not the Category Thesis. The introduction of mandatory prescription retention led to an initial ~16% drop in daily unit sales (from ~20k to ~17k units/day), but volumes quickly recovered, reaching ~21–22k units/day following new product launches. Pre-regulation baseline was ~15–16k units/day. Importantly, the number of prescribing physicians surged from ~15–16k per month to ~130–135k, indicating formalization of demand rather than contraction. While a parallel market may have emerged, the data suggests that regulation did not materially disrupt category growth. 
*	Prescription Breadth Is Expanding Rapidly. Physician adoption has been exceptionally fast, with >50% of prescribers now writing for tirzepatide and ~22% prescribing both tirzepatide and semaglutide. Within months of launch, ~20–25% of HCPs had already adopted the new therapy, accelerating to majority adoption within a year. The prescriber base expanded nearly 10x (from ~15k to >130k), extending beyond endocrinology into general practitioners, cardiologists, and other specialties. This broadening of the prescriber base supports sustained volume growth and deeper market penetration. 
*	Consumer Basket Effects Support a Chronic Care Angle. GLP-1 users exhibit significantly higher co-purchase rates across chronic therapies, including anti-hypertensives, cholesterol medications, diabetes treatments, and thyroid therapies. This aligns with the ~30%+ comorbidity rates observed in obese populations and reinforces the category’s role in driving broader pharmaceutical consumption. The implication is that each GLP-1 patient represents a multi-category revenue opportunity, increasing lifetime value beyond the core therapy. 
*	Seasonality and Aesthetic Consumption Are Emerging Considerations. Sales exhibit seasonality, with monthly peaks reaching ~US$270m during summer months (Nov–Dec) and declining into January. This suggests that part of demand is linked to aesthetic usage rather than purely chronic treatment. Monthly sales volatility and post-summer drop-offs indicate a potential mix of continuous and intermittent users. While not undermining the structural growth story, this introduces variability in quarterly trends and highlights a behavioral component in demand. 
*	Pricing, Accessibility, and New Entrants Could Broaden the Market Further. Current pricing remains elevated (~R$1,000–2,000/month, or ~US$150–400/month), limiting access primarily to higher-income consumers. However, upcoming generic entry (e.g., semaglutide post-patent expiry), local production, and increased competition are likely to drive price reductions. As affordability improves, the addressable market could expand significantly beyond the current premium segment. 
*	Retailer Bargaining Power and Category Dependence. GLP-1 penetration within large chains is estimated to be significantly higher than the market average (~9–10% of sales vs. ~4.7% overall), underscoring their importance as a revenue driver. This concentration enhances retailer bargaining power and makes supply continuity critical. Given the category’s contribution to growth and margins, leading chains are likely to prioritize allocation and maintain strong influence over supplier negotiations. 
*	Promotion Model Remains Highly Regulated. The promotional environment is tightly regulated, with no allowance for financial incentives to prescribers. Adoption has therefore been driven by clinical efficacy, patient demand, and medical education. The absence of sampling (due to high product cost) further accelerates direct monetization, as patients must immediately purchase upon prescription. This dynamic reinforces the quality of demand and reduces the risk of artificially stimulated uptake. 
*	Pipeline Optionality and the Next Innovation Wave. The global pipeline remains active, with ongoing investments in next-generation obesity therapies, including oral formulations and combination mechanisms. While current oral GLP-1 penetration remains limited in Brazil, future innovation could expand modality options and improve adherence. M&A activity and partnerships highlight the strategic importance of the category globally, suggesting continued innovation and competitive intensity.
