Meeting the outward-bound CEO

Breakfast meeting with Mario Leão after last week´s announcement

Santander's IR team organized a breakfast meeting between the CEO, Mario Leão, and the sell-side earlier today, as they usually do once or twice a year. This time around, the meeting gained in relevance given that Mario is leaving the company in mid-2026, with the CEO of B3 Gilson Finkelsztain taking the helm. Gustavo Alejo, who is also leaving Santander (after 26 years) and the CFO position in April, joined in as well, together with Spaniard Carlos Gonzalez-Blanch (new CFO). Mario described his departure as a personal decision, saying that turning 50 made him re-think his priorities/objectives. He said he is open to working with Gilson Finkelsztain on a good transition, though they haven't spoken yet. He mentioned that the leadership change does not imply any strategic shift. According to him, the bank has a clear strategy centered on reducing earnings cyclicality, increasing the weight of retail funding, and making fee income more relevant. Mario reiterated his conviction that Santander Brasil can deliver ROE above 20%, ideally by 2028 (in line with Santander Group’s guidance), although 2026 in particular should be more challenging.

No change in strategy, but 2026 should still be a transition year

Mario was emphatic that the CEO change has nothing to do with any shift in direction. In his view, the bank has “never been so well set up,” and results should become increasingly predictable as execution advances. Even so, management acknowledged that 2026 should still be somewhat challenging. H1 could be a bit more pressured by higher provisions, partly due to some pressures seen in 4Q25 coupled with some specific corporate cases, although the message was that there should be no material jump in cost of risk. We understood as well that some of the provisions related to high-profile corporate cases, for instance, should be recognized gradually, and there is some expectation that the restructuring processes these companies are undergoing will effectively improve their repayment capacity. Looking ahead, in the medium term, however, the expectation is that a less risky portfolio could allow for some improvement in cost of risk, or at least stability, with provisions growing in line with the loan book.

Growth should remain selective, with better opportunities in chosen segments

On the top line, the CEO said Santander should continue to grow the portfolio below system growth in 2026, as it has in recent years, with a selective approach. The bank wants to gain share mainly in segments it considers strategic, such as affluent clients, SMEs, and mid-sized corporates. These segments should grow at double-digit rates and contribute disproportionately over time. SMEs remain particularly attractive in terms of profitability, although management acknowledged that NPL risk has been somewhat higher and therefore requires caution. By contrast, Santander is not focused on gaining market share in lower-income retail or very large corporates. The view on the low-income segment remains more cautious, with part of the portfolio in run-off, particularly legacy and partnership-driven exposures. In large corporates, credit ROE stands at around 14–15%, and new deals are only pursued when there is clear cross-sell potential. Otherwise, the bank prefers to rely on capital markets solutions or focus on shorter-duration transactions.

Private payroll loans still look like an attractive opportunity, but Santander is more cautious for now

Management continues to like the structural thesis of private payroll loans, especially when combined with a deeper client relationship. Santander noted that the best-performing customers are those with a broader banking relationship, including current accounts, cards, and Pix activity. That said, the bank made it clear that it is currently more conservative than peers. The new product is operationally very different to the old model, with more participants and greater uncertainty around execution. Santander also highlighted that the FGTS protection layer is still under discussion with the government and not yet fully in place. More importantly, management said that when origination accelerated, early-stage delinquencies came in above expectations. As a result, while the bank remains constructive on the opportunity, it wants more time before becoming more aggressive.

Market NII should lose relevance over time; costs remain a key pillar of the story

Management also reinforced that market NII should become structurally less relevant over time, as the bank increases hedging and reduces directional exposure. That said, the key message is that it is more appropriate to look at NII on a consolidated basis, and management intends to provide more clarity on this going forward. Lower interest rates should help market NII become less negative than last year, but management does not expect this to drive a material improvement in the bottom line. Expenses remain a key lever in the medium-term story. Mario argued that Santander has already been doing a better job than its peers in cost control, with expenses growing below inflation in recent years. He also highlighted meaningful efficiency gains from migrating legacy systems to the cloud. Over time, management aims to keep expenses growth close to zero.

Taxes are the main headwind below the line

A key point from the discussion was the tax burden. Management said the effective tax rate should rise in 2026 and increase again in 2027. As a result, EBT will need to grow more materially just to offset this pressure at the net income level. Management sees this as important, as it should allow Santander to gradually utilize its DTAs, which remain sizable on the balance sheet and are currently a relative disadvantage versus peers such as Itaú and BTG—a point mentioned during the discussion. In any case, despite DTAs being a headwind for capital ratios in the short term, the message was that there is no risk to cut the 50% payout.

Weak momentum this year; Neutral reiterated

It was a good catch-up, and a very timely one given Mario’s resignation announcement at the end of last week. We thank him for keeping the meeting and for holding these breakfast conversations regularly during his tenure as CEO. Indeed, it was right after a meeting with him in October 2023 that we found the confidence to upgrade the stock from Sell to Neutral (see: “Signs of an early recovery <https://www.btgpactual.com/research/report-view/99383> ”), which proved to be the right call. Looking at stock performance this year, shares have underperformed peers, which in our view reflects a more challenging earnings outlook for 2026—consistent with the message we highlight in this note. We thank Mario for his engagement over the past few years and hope to remain in touch going forward. We also look forward to hearing from Gilson (new CEO) on how he views the bank, its opportunities, and challenges. For now, we maintain our Neutral rating.
